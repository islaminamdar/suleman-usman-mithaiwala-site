PART THREE — RESOURCES

# The authority and regulator directory

*Who governs what — and when in your journey each one matters.*

---

A Dubai business does not answer to a single office. It answers to a small set of authorities, each with its own remit, and the order in which you meet them is largely fixed by the setup sequence in Chapter 3 and the launch plan in this part. This directory is a map of that set: for each authority, what it governs and the point at which a founder needs it. Use it to know in advance which door a given decision belongs behind, so that an approval is never discovered late.

One caveat governs the entire directory, and it is not a formality. The names of departments, the structure of portals, the location of service centres, and the published contact routes all change — some have changed more than once in recent years. This directory deliberately does not print phone numbers or counter addresses, because a wrong number is worse than none. For every authority below, reach it through its current official website or its official app, and confirm any procedure or fee directly before you act on it.

## The authorities you will almost certainly meet

**DET — Dubai Department of Economy and Tourism.** Formerly the DED. DET licenses every mainland company in Dubai and governs mainland trade names, initial approvals, activity lists, and licence renewals. If your jurisdiction decision points to the mainland, DET is the issuing authority for your licence and the office you renew with each year. Its Trakheesi system handles many mainland permits and approvals. You need DET from the moment you choose mainland, and every year thereafter at renewal.

**The free zone authorities.** Dubai has 40-plus free zones, and each one is its own licensing authority — DMCC, IFZA, Meydan, Dubai South, DIFC, Dubai CommerCity, Dubai Internet City, and many more. If you set up in a free zone, that zone's authority is your equivalent of DET: it issues your licence, registers your name, provides your premises or flexi-desk, and in most cases processes your establishment card and visa applications through its own one-stop channel. You need your chosen free zone authority from jurisdiction selection through to every renewal. The DIFC additionally operates its own independent legal and regulatory framework, which matters if you set up there.

**GDRFA — General Directorate of Residency and Foreigners Affairs.** GDRFA governs entry permits, residence visas, and the residency status of you and everyone you sponsor. Every founder who intends to live in the UAE, and every business that intends to employ resident staff, deals with GDRFA — directly on the mainland, or via the free zone's channel inside a zone. You need GDRFA once your establishment card is issued and the visa stage begins, and again at every visa renewal.

**The FTA — Federal Tax Authority.** The FTA administers both VAT, in force at 5% since 2018, and the federal corporate tax of 9% on profits above AED 375,000. It handles tax registration, returns, and deregistration. You need the FTA after your licence is issued: for corporate tax registration as required, and for VAT registration once your taxable supplies meet the mandatory threshold or you choose to register voluntarily. Confirm current thresholds and deadlines with the FTA directly — these have moved before and may move again.

## The authorities you meet if your activity calls for them

Not every founder deals with every authority below. Which ones apply to you is decided by your activity — which is why Chapter 3 insists you fix your activity wording early.

**MOHRE — Ministry of Human Resources and Emiratisation.** MOHRE governs labour and employment for mainland companies: employment contracts, work permits, the WPS payroll system, and Emiratisation obligations. Its Tasheel service centres process much of this work. A mainland employer needs MOHRE from the first hire onward. Free zone companies handle employment through their own zone authority rather than MOHRE; confirm which framework applies to you.

**Dubai Municipality.** The Municipality governs food safety, public health, and building and environmental permits. Any business handling food — restaurant, café, cloud kitchen, catering, bakery — needs Municipality approval for its premises and food licence. Salons, spas, and other health-adjacent businesses face health permits here too. You need the Municipality during the premises and approvals phase, before you may trade.

**Dubai Civil Defence.** Civil Defence governs fire safety and life safety: fit-out approvals, fire systems, and the inspections that clear a physical premises for occupation. Any founder fitting out a shop, kitchen, clinic, studio, or warehouse needs Civil Defence sign-off before opening, and it is among the most common causes of delay — begin it as early as the fit-out allows.

**Dubai Land Department and RERA.** The DLD regulates real estate, and RERA — the Real Estate Regulatory Agency within it — regulates the profession: brokerage, broker permits, the broker examination, and property management. A real estate brokerage or property manager needs the DLD and RERA before trading. Any founder signing a commercial tenancy also meets the DLD's Ejari system, which registers the lease.

**The RTA — Roads and Transport Authority.** The RTA governs vehicles, transport, and mobility permits — relevant to car rental, chauffeur and limousine services, delivery and last-mile logistics, and any business operating vehicles for hire. If your activity puts vehicles on the road commercially, you need RTA permits before you operate. Salik, the road-toll system, sits under the RTA's wider remit.

**DTCM — Dubai's tourism authority within DET.** DTCM governs tourism activity: tour operators, travel agencies, desert safari operators, hotels, and holiday-home rentals. It also administers the Tourism Dirham levy on guest stays. A tourism, travel, or holiday-homes founder needs DTCM approval as part of licensing.

**KHDA — Knowledge and Human Development Authority.** KHDA governs private education and training: schools, nurseries, training institutes, and tutoring centres. If your activity is education or training, KHDA approval is part of your licensing and is required before you may enrol students.

## How to use this directory

Read your chosen Part Two industry chapter alongside this page. Section 3 of each chapter — *Licence and jurisdiction* — names the specific approvals that industry triggers. Cross-reference those named approvals here to understand what each authority governs and when, in the 90-day plan, you will need it. Then, and only then, confirm the current procedure and any fee with that authority through its official channel. The map tells you which door to knock on; the authority behind it sets the rules, and those rules are theirs to change.
