# Free zone vs mainland — the comparison

*The decision that sets your costs, your customers, and your licence in a single stroke.*

---

This is the first structural decision a Dubai founder makes, and it shapes every decision after it. Chapter 2 made the case in full; this resource lays the three options side by side so you can choose with the facts in front of you. There is no universally correct answer. There is only the answer that fits where your customers are, who they are, and what you intend to do.

## The three options, plainly

**Mainland.** A mainland company is licensed by DET — the Dubai Department of Economy and Tourism — and may trade directly anywhere in the UAE market: to the public, to other businesses, in shops and offices across the city, and with government bodies. For the large majority of commercial and industrial activities, 100% foreign ownership has been permitted since the 2021 reform; a limited set of strategic activities still carries ownership restrictions, so confirm your specific activity. The mainland is the natural home of any business whose customer is the UAE public or whose work requires an unrestricted local presence.

**Free zone.** A free zone company is licensed by one of Dubai's 40-plus free zone authorities, each with its own regime, activity list, and pricing. Full foreign ownership has always been the rule. A free zone company trades freely within its own zone and internationally, and a qualifying free zone person may, under specific conditions, retain a 0% corporate tax rate on qualifying income. The traditional limit is that selling directly into the mainland UAE market is not as straightforward as it is for a mainland company — it is typically done through a mainland distributor, a branch, or other arrangements. Free zones suit businesses serving international or business clients, and many offer fast, packaged, predictable setup.

**Offshore.** An offshore company — for example JAFZA Offshore or RAK ICC — is a holding and structuring vehicle. It is used for holding shares, owning intellectual property, owning property where permitted, and international structuring. It has no UAE operating presence and grants no residence visas. It is not a way to run a trading business in Dubai; it is a way to hold one.

## Comparison table

| Factor | Mainland | Free zone | Offshore |
|---|---|---|---|
| Licensing authority | DET | One of 40-plus free zone authorities | Offshore registry (e.g. JAFZA, RAK ICC) |
| Foreign ownership | 100% for most activities; some strategic activities restricted | 100%, always | 100% |
| Where you may trade | Anywhere in the UAE, including to the public and government | Within the zone and internationally; into the mainland via a distributor or branch | No UAE trading; holding and structuring only |
| Residence visas | Yes, subject to office space and quotas | Yes, subject to the package and facility | None |
| Premises | Real office or unit usually required; address must be registered | Flexi-desk to full unit, depending on package | Not applicable |
| Corporate tax | 9% above AED 375,000; 0% below | 9% standard; 0% on qualifying income for a qualifying free zone person | Subject to its own treatment; confirm |
| Audit | Required for some activities; confirm | Required by many zones | Often required by the registry |
| Cost shape | Variable; driven by activity, approvals, and real premises | Often a packaged, predictable headline price | Low and predictable; a structuring fee, not an operating cost |
| Banking perception | Generally well understood by banks | Well understood; some zones carry stronger reputations | Can attract more compliance scrutiny |

Every figure and rule above is indicative and subject to change. Confirm the current position with the relevant authority before you decide.

## The main types of free zone

"Free zone" is not one thing. Dubai's zones cluster into a few broad types, and matching the type to your business matters as much as the price.

**Commodity and trading zones.** Zones built around trade and commodities — DMCC is the best known — suit general trading, commodities, and businesses that value a strong, bank-friendly reputation.

**Sector and industry zones.** Zones organised around an industry — media, technology, healthcare, design, education — bring a relevant community, tailored activity lists, and sometimes sector regulators on site. A media or tech founder often benefits from being inside the right cluster.

**Logistics and industrial zones.** Zones attached to ports, airports, and industrial land — Jebel Ali and Dubai South among them — suit manufacturing, warehousing, and logistics, where proximity to freight infrastructure is the asset.

**Financial zones.** The DIFC operates under its own legal and regulatory framework and suits financial services, funds, and firms that want a common-law environment.

**General-purpose, low-cost zones.** Several zones — IFZA and Meydan are common examples — compete on price, speed, and a broad activity list, and suit lean service businesses and first-time founders who want a straightforward, predictable package.

## How to choose a free zone

If a free zone is right for you, choose the specific one on five tests, in order. **Activity:** does the zone license your exact activity? This is a veto — no further questions if the answer is no. **True cost:** compare the genuine first-year total, including visas, the establishment card, and the desk, not the advertised licence. **Premises:** does the package give you the space and visa quota you actually need? **Reputation:** some zones are better recognised by banks and clients, which eases account opening and credibility. **Location and community:** physical convenience and being among relevant peers both have real value.

## The decision guide

Strip the decision to one question: **where are your customers, and who are they?**

If your customers are the **UAE public** — a restaurant, salon, clinic, retail shop, or any business needing to serve walk-in or local customers directly — choose **mainland**. The same applies if you need to bid for government work or require an unrestricted local footprint.

If your customers are **international or business clients** served from an office or remotely — consultancy, software, media, trading with a focus on export and re-export, agencies — a **free zone** is often the better fit, for full ownership, packaged setup, and the qualifying-income tax position.

If you intend to **sell into the UAE market from a free zone**, plan from the start for a distributor, a mainland branch, or a dual-licence arrangement, and cost it in. Do not assume the mainland sale is free.

If you are **holding assets, shares, or intellectual property** with no UAE operations and no need for visas, an **offshore** company is the right and economical tool — and only for that.

Choose for the next three years, not the next three weeks. Switching jurisdictions later is possible but rarely cheap. The right first choice is the one that matches the customer you are actually building the business to serve.
