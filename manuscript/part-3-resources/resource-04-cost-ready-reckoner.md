PART THREE — RESOURCES

# The cost ready-reckoner

*The line items behind any launch budget — and how to build one that is yours.*

---

Every Part Two chapter gave you a capital range for one industry. This resource shows you the parts that make up any such range, so that you can build a budget from the ground rather than borrow one. A range you have assembled yourself, line by line, is a budget you can defend to a bank, a partner, and — most importantly — to yourself at month eight, when the early optimism has worn off and only the numbers remain.

Read this page with one fact held firmly in mind. Every figure here is **indicative**: a realistic order of magnitude to plan against, not a quotation. Costs in Dubai vary by activity, by jurisdiction, by the specific free zone, by the size of premises, and by the year. They move. Treat each range below as a starting estimate, then confirm the current figure for your exact situation with the relevant authority, using the directory in this part. A budget built on indicative ranges and then verified is sound. A budget built on a single advertised headline is not.

## The cost lines a founder should budget for

**The licence.** The headline number, and the smallest part of the truth. A basic free zone licence is advertised from roughly AED 12,500 a year; mainland licence costs vary more widely with activity and approvals. *Indicative: AED 12,500–30,000 a year for many small businesses, with some activities materially higher.* Budget the licence as one line among many, never as the cost of opening.

**Registration and approvals.** Trade name reservation, initial approval, the memorandum of association or equivalent, and any activity-specific external approvals — Dubai Municipality, Dubai Civil Defence, DTCM, KHDA, RERA and others. *Indicative: AED 2,000–15,000 in total, depending heavily on how many external approvals your activity triggers.* A consultancy may sit at the bottom of this range; a restaurant or clinic well above it.

**The establishment card.** Also called the immigration card — the document that lets your company sponsor visas. *Indicative: AED 1,000–2,500, with periodic renewal.* Small, but non-negotiable: nothing on the visa side begins without it.

**Residence visas.** Entry permit, status change, stamping, and the visa itself, for the founder and each employee. *Indicative: AED 3,500–7,000 per person, per visa cycle.* A solo founder budgets one; a business with staff multiplies this line by every head, and it is among the most commonly underestimated costs in a launch.

**Emirates ID and medical fitness tests.** Each visa requires a medical fitness test and an Emirates ID. *Indicative: AED 700–1,500 per person combined.* Modest individually; budget it for everyone you sponsor.

**Premises and Ejari.** The registered address your licence requires. A flexi-desk or shared-desk package may satisfy a service business; a shop, clinic, kitchen, or warehouse needs real premises and an Ejari-registered tenancy. *Indicative: AED 12,000–25,000 a year for a flexi-desk or small shared office; AED 40,000–150,000-plus a year for a real commercial unit, by size and location.* Remember the Dubai rhythm from Chapter 1: rent is often paid annually in advance, in one or two cheques, plus a security deposit and agency fee. Budget the whole year's rent as a day-one outflow, not a monthly one.

**Fit-out.** The cost of making premises fit to trade — design, construction, fixtures, signage, and Civil Defence-compliant systems. *Indicative: from a few thousand dirhams for a light office refresh to AED 150,000–500,000-plus for a restaurant kitchen or a clinic.* This line varies more than any other; the figure in your Part Two chapter is the better guide for your industry.

**Insurance.** Cover appropriate to your activity — public liability, professional indemnity, property, and the medical insurance you are obliged to provide to sponsored staff. *Indicative: AED 1,500–10,000 a year for a small business, more with employees and higher-risk activities.*

**Accounting and bookkeeping.** Engaging a bookkeeper or accountant from day one, as Chapter 4 and the launch plan both insist. *Indicative: AED 6,000–24,000 a year for a small business, depending on transaction volume and whether an audit is required.* Clean books are far cheaper to maintain than to reconstruct.

**Working capital.** The line founders most often omit, and the one that decides whether the business survives its first year. This is the money that funds operations — stock, salaries, rent, marketing, utilities — before the business reliably pays for itself. It is not a setup fee; it is the fuel. Size it from your runway, not from a rule of thumb.

## Building a realistic first-year budget

Add the lines above for your specific business, using the figures in your Part Two chapter to sharpen the ranges. Then apply three disciplines.

**Separate setup costs from running costs.** Setup costs — licence, registration, establishment card, first visas, fit-out — are one-off and largely front-loaded. Running costs — rent, salaries, insurance, accounting, marketing, utilities — recur every month whether or not a customer has appeared. A founder who counts only the first group has budgeted to open a business, not to operate one.

**Add a contingency.** Approvals take longer than planned, a quote comes in higher, an inspection requires a change. A contingency of 15–20% over your assembled total is prudence, not pessimism. If it is not spent, it becomes runway.

**Plan the runway openly.** Chapter 1 set the standard: a cash runway covering both the business and your own living costs across six to twelve months is the plan, not the worst case. Most businesses in this book take that long to reach a steady footing. Calculate your monthly running cost, multiply by your honest months-to-breakeven, and treat that figure as a budget line as real as the licence.

## A worked shape

For a lean, single-founder service business in a free zone, a defensible first-year total often lands somewhere around **AED 60,000–120,000** once licence, one visa, a flexi-desk, insurance, accounting, and a modest working-capital cushion are counted. For a premises-based business — a restaurant, salon, clinic, or retail shop — the first-year figure is a different order entirely, frequently **several hundred thousand dirhams**, driven by fit-out, real rent paid in advance, equipment, stock, and a larger payroll.

These two shapes are illustrative, not quotes. Build your own from the lines above, verify each figure against the current position, and you will hold a budget you can stand behind. Profit, as Chapter 1 put it, is an opinion; cash is a fact. The ready-reckoner exists so that the fact does not surprise you.
