PART TWO — THE FIELD GUIDE  ·  HOME & PROPERTY SERVICES

# Chapter 18 · Handyman & Maintenance

*Unglamorous, essential, and never short of work — the business of keeping buildings running across one of the world's fastest-growing city footprints.*

---

## 1 · What this business actually is in Dubai

A handyman and maintenance business in Dubai provides repair, upkeep, and minor-works services to residential and commercial properties: electrical faults, plumbing problems, air-conditioning servicing, carpentry repairs, painting, tiling, and the dozens of small but urgent jobs that arise in any occupied building. At the simpler end, a handyman resolves single-trade snags for an individual homeowner. At the more structured end, a maintenance company holds annual maintenance contracts (AMCs) with residential buildings, communities, or commercial clients, providing scheduled servicing and rapid response across multiple trades.

Dubai's property stock creates consistent structural demand. Air-conditioning systems operate under extreme load for the majority of the year and require servicing and repair routinely. Buildings age, tenants turn over, and developers deliver units that need minor rectifications before habitation. The city's pace of construction means there is always a new layer of properties entering the maintenance cycle.

What makes this sector distinct from a purely informal handyman market is its regulatory character. Building maintenance activities in Dubai fall under a defined category of licensed work, and qualified technical staff are central to the proposition. Clients — particularly corporate ones — expect to see evidence of qualified technicians, relevant insurance, and appropriate registration before awarding contracts. The informal end of the market exists. It is not the basis for a defensible business.

The typical business model for a small-to-mid-sized operator combines AMC revenue — fixed annual or monthly fees for defined servicing obligations — with reactive call-out work charged at a day or half-day rate. AMC revenue is predictable. Call-outs are higher-margin but variable. The most stable operators optimise for AMC coverage first and treat reactive work as upside.

## 2 · Capital required

The capital structure of this business depends significantly on which trades you cover and how many technicians you employ. A focused single-trade operation — say, air-conditioning service only — has a simpler capital profile than a multi-trade maintenance company.

**Licence and approvals** begin with a DET commercial licence for building maintenance or general maintenance activities. The licence category matters: ensure the activity description covers the trades you intend to offer. Budget AED 12,000–20,000 for the licence, establishment card, and related fees, and confirm current figures with DET before filing.

**Qualified technicians and visas** are the core asset. A minimum viable team might be two technicians plus a coordinator. Each technician requires a work visa, Emirates ID, medical tests, and — depending on the trade — trade certifications or test results. Budget AED 25,000–45,000 per employee in first-year visa and establishment costs, including the government fees and any overseas recruitment expenses.

**Equipment and tools** for a small multi-trade team — basic electrical, plumbing, and carpentry toolsets, plus an air-conditioning testing and gas-handling kit — run AED 20,000–40,000 at the outset.

**Transport** is non-negotiable. Technicians need to reach the job, often with equipment. A secondhand van or pick-up suitable for a small team costs AED 30,000–55,000 purchased outright, or AED 2,000–3,500 per month on a lease.

**Accommodation** may be required for workers who cannot independently rent in Dubai. Factor this in early if applicable.

**Working capital** must carry wages, transport, and overheads until AMC billings and reactive income reach a consistent level.

A lean start with two technicians: AED 150,000–220,000. A comfortable multi-trade operation with four or five staff: AED 280,000–400,000. Verify all figures; they move.

## 3 · Licence & jurisdiction

**DET commercial licence:** The standard route is a mainland licence from the Dubai Department of Economy and Tourism, with the activity listed as building maintenance, general maintenance, or a specific trade activity depending on the scope of services. The activity description must accurately reflect what your technicians will do — an activity that does not list the relevant trade will create problems when a client reviews your paperwork.

**Dubai Municipality approvals:** For certain maintenance works — particularly anything that touches building structure, electrical systems in commercial buildings, or mechanical plant — Dubai Municipality may require contractor registration or technical approval. At the time of writing, small-scope residential maintenance typically does not require separate DM contractor approval, but commercial maintenance contracts, particularly in multi-storey buildings, increasingly require it. Confirm the current requirement for your specific trade and client profile before tendering.

**Qualified technical staff:** MOHRE and DET both have provisions related to technical activities requiring qualified personnel. Certain electrical and mechanical works require practitioners to hold relevant trade certificates or qualifications. Clients in the commercial and facilities management space will ask for this documentation as a matter of course. Do not staff your technical roles with unqualified workers and assume it will not surface. It will.

**Free zone vs mainland:** A free zone entity is not well-suited to a business that is physically performing services across the Dubai mainland. A mainland DET licence is the correct structure.

**Practical recommendation:** Start with the DET licence, define your activity scope precisely, and begin building your technical compliance documentation from day one. Corporate clients and property managers will require it before they award work.

## 4 · First three customers

**Property managers and building supervisors.** The fastest path to an AMC contract is through the person who manages a building. Every residential tower, compound, and commercial property has a property manager who is either directly responsible for maintenance or who advises the owner. A direct introduction — through a shared contact, a community event, or a straightforward cold approach with your licence and a brief capability statement — can produce a trial contract for one building. One building leads to referrals to other buildings managed by the same company.

**Real estate agents and landlords.** Individual landlords in Dubai often manage multiple units and need a reliable maintenance contact they can trust and call at short notice. Real estate agents who manage rentals are a natural referral source: they encounter maintenance problems constantly and welcome a recommendation they can make to their landlord clients. An introduction to three active property agents is worth more than a social media advertisement.

**Developers' snagging programmes.** New developments regularly require snagging services — the systematic identification and rectification of minor defects before handover to buyers. Positioning your team to deliver snagging work for a developer or their warranty team provides a concentrated source of work, builds familiarity with new communities, and generates direct referrals from residents who see your quality at handover.

These three channels — building managers, landlords and agents, and developers — represent the realistic pipeline for your first three contracts, and for many contracts thereafter.

## 5 · Profit margins & cash cycle

AMC contracts typically carry gross margins of 35–50% depending on the scope, the frequency of scheduled visits, and the materials required. The margin is earned over the life of the contract rather than in a single transaction, and it depends on your technicians actually delivering the scheduled visits without running over on call-outs that were included in the AMC price.

Reactive call-out work — charged at a day rate or a fixed fee per job — carries higher gross margins, often 45–60%, but is variable and unpredictable. A business built entirely on reactive work is exposed to revenue swings that make planning difficult.

Net margin after wages, vehicle costs, accommodation, equipment depreciation, and overheads typically sits at 12–20% for a well-run small operation. The greatest margin risk is scope creep on AMC contracts: a contract priced to cover four visits per year can become unprofitable if every visit uncovers problems that take twice as long to resolve as budgeted.

**The cash cycle** is more manageable than in pure B2B businesses, because AMC contracts are often invoiced in advance — quarterly or annually — and reactive jobs are typically paid within a shorter window. The risk is B2B commercial clients who operate on 45–60 day terms. If your portfolio is weighted toward commercial clients, plan for this gap. Invoice on time and chase systematically.

Breakeven for a two- to three-person operation with modest overheads typically requires three to five AMC contracts covering mid-size residential buildings, plus a steady flow of reactive call-outs. Expect six to nine months to reach that position from a standing start.

## 6 · Top 3 mistakes

**Pricing AMC contracts without fully costing the call-out obligations.** The most common financial trap in this business is an AMC priced on the assumption that scheduled visits are the only time commitment, when in practice the contract also includes an obligation to respond to reactive faults. Founders who price AMCs based on the scheduled servicing cost alone — without modelling the realistic frequency of reactive call-outs — find that the most active buildings consume far more technician-hours than the contract covers. Before you price any AMC, model the worst-case call-out scenario and ensure the contract rate covers it.

**Overstating technical capabilities to win the first contract.** The pressure to win early contracts leads some operators to represent their team as covering trades or qualifications they do not yet have. When the job requires a skill the team lacks, the outcome is either a botched repair or an emergency subcontract at a cost that exceeds the fee. Either way, the client does not return. Start with the trades you can genuinely deliver, build competence methodically, and add trades as your team grows. A smaller, honest scope wins repeat business. An overstated scope destroys it.

**Ignoring the insurance and liability exposure.** A maintenance company operating in occupied homes and commercial buildings carries real liability: a poorly executed electrical repair can cause a fire; a plumbing error can flood the floor below. Professional indemnity and public liability insurance are not bureaucratic formalities. They are the difference between a manageable incident and a business-ending claim. Ensure your cover is current, adequate for the scale of contracts you are pursuing, and presented to corporate clients before they ask for it.

## 7 · Who's already winning

The handyman and maintenance operators winning in Dubai today divide into two camps that rarely compete with each other. The first is the large integrated FM company holding multi-building contracts with developers and real estate funds, delivering maintenance as part of a bundled facilities services package. These firms win on scale, financial stability, and the ability to absorb contractual risk. A start-up operator cannot take them on directly.

The second winning position is the mid-sized specialist: a maintenance company known for a specific trade — air-conditioning, for example — or a specific community, where they are the default call for every building manager in the area. These operators succeed because reliability, not price, is what property managers actually buy. A building manager who has been let down by three maintenance companies in a row will pay a premium for one that answers the phone, shows up on time, and does not leave a job half-finished.

The struggling operators are those who cover too many trades with too few qualified staff, who win contracts by underbidding and then fail to deliver, and who have not solved the scheduling and transport logistics that are the real operational challenge. The gap between a licence and a functioning maintenance operation is large, and the gap between a functioning operation and a profitable one is larger still.

---

## 8 · Verdict

Handyman and maintenance services in Dubai is a business with durable, structural demand and a clear path to recurring revenue through AMC contracts. The city's size and its pace of construction mean there is no shortage of buildings that need servicing. But the business is harder to run profitably than it looks, because margin depends entirely on qualified people arriving on time, doing the job correctly, and not creating problems that cost more to fix than the original contract was worth.

This suits the operator who has technical knowledge of at least one trade, who understands how to manage a mobile field workforce, and who is prepared to build a compliance posture — licences, insurance, qualified staff documentation — before the first corporate contract is needed.

It does not suit the founder who plans to figure out the technical side as they go, who has underestimated staff costs, or who intends to compete on price with informal operators. The informal market exists; it is not a business.

**Bottom line:** A technically credible, well-capitalised maintenance company with a focus on AMC revenue and one or two anchor communities can build a stable and scalable business — but only if the technical and compliance foundations are laid before the first contract is signed.
