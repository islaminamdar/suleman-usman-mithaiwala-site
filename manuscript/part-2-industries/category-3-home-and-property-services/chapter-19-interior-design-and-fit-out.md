# Chapter 19 · Interior Design & Fit-Out

*Two related but legally distinct activities — one sells ideas and the other builds them — and the difference matters from the moment you file your licence.*

---

## 1 · What this business actually is in Dubai

Interior design and fit-out refers to the spectrum of services between imagining how a space should look and actually constructing it. In practice, the market contains two distinct activities that are often confused and must be treated separately from a licensing perspective.

**Interior design** is the professional advisory service: space planning, concept development, material and finish specification, furniture selection, lighting design, and the production of drawings and schedules for a client to use. The output is intellectual and consultative. An interior designer may specify a marble floor; they do not necessarily lay it.

**Fit-out contracting** is the physical execution: construction, joinery, MEP (mechanical, electrical, and plumbing) rough-ins, raised flooring, suspended ceilings, and the full build-out of a space. A fit-out contractor takes a shell space and delivers a finished, habitable interior. The output is a built environment, and the contractor is responsible for it structurally, mechanically, and in terms of fire safety.

In Dubai, these are different licensed activities with different regulatory requirements. Some businesses hold both — a design-and-build firm that conceives and constructs — but each component must be properly licenced.

The market is driven by Dubai's relentless cycle of new construction, turnover of commercial tenants, and the cultural appetite for high-quality residential interiors. Hotel refurbishments, retail fit-outs, restaurant launches, office relocations, and luxury villa renovations are all live project types. The commercial sector — particularly F&B, retail, and hospitality — tends to produce higher-value contracts and tighter deadlines.

What makes Dubai distinctive is the pace and the expectation of quality. Clients expect projects delivered to an international standard, on a timeline that is aggressive by any measure, in a market where material lead times and subcontractor availability can both surprise an unprepared operator.

## 2 · Capital required

The capital structure differs significantly depending on whether you are starting as a design consultancy, a fit-out contractor, or a design-and-build firm.

**A design consultancy** is relatively asset-light. You need a licence, an office or co-working space, design software licences (typically AutoCAD, SketchUp, and a rendering tool), and the salaries of your designers. An experienced senior designer commands AED 10,000–20,000 per month. A lean two-person design practice can start for AED 120,000–180,000, with working capital being the primary consideration while the first project billings come in.

**A fit-out contracting business** is more capital-intensive. You need not only the licence but also a site team, tools, materials floated ahead of client payment milestones, and — for most commercial jobs — a performance bond or bank guarantee. The materials and subcontractor float is the key capital risk: on a large fit-out project, you may spend AED 500,000 in material and labour before the client's first milestone payment arrives.

**Licence and approvals:** Budget AED 15,000–25,000 for the DET commercial licence. For fit-out contracting, add the cost of Dubai Municipality contractor registration and Civil Defence approvals (see Section 3), which will add to your first-year regulatory spend. Confirm current figures directly with the relevant authorities.

**Office and soft costs:** Even a fit-out company needs a credible commercial address, a project management platform, and a presentation capability for pitching. Budget AED 40,000–70,000 for office rent in a mid-tier location.

**Staff:** A site manager, a project coordinator, and access to reliable trade subcontractors are the minimum operating structure for a fit-out firm. Each employee requires visa and onboarding costs of AED 15,000–25,000 in the first year.

A lean design consultancy: AED 120,000–200,000. A properly capitalised design-and-build firm: AED 350,000–600,000. These ranges are indicative; verify everything before committing.

## 3 · Licence & jurisdiction

The licensing distinction between design and fit-out is one of the most important facts in this chapter.

**Interior design licence:** A DET commercial licence listing interior design as the activity covers the advisory, consultative, and specification function. This licence permits the design work but does not authorise you to carry out physical construction or fit-out work.

**Fit-out contracting licence:** If you intend to execute physical construction — carpentry, MEP, raised flooring, partitioning, ceilings — you need a contracting licence. This is a different activity category from interior design, and it carries additional requirements.

**Dubai Municipality contractor registration:** Fit-out contractors performing work in Dubai must be registered with Dubai Municipality as an approved contractor. The registration process includes demonstrating qualified technical personnel, relevant insurance, and compliance with technical standards. Without DM registration, you cannot legally execute commercial fit-out work in Dubai. The requirements and fees are set by DM and are subject to revision; confirm the current process directly.

**Civil Defence approval:** In Dubai, fit-out works in commercial and hospitality spaces require Civil Defence sign-off — specifically, approval of the MEP design and, upon completion, a compliance certificate confirming that fire detection, suppression, and egress requirements are met. A fit-out contractor who delivers a project without Civil Defence approval leaves the client unable to obtain a trade licence or occupancy permit for the space. This is not a technicality; it is a hard requirement.

**Free zone vs mainland:** Design consultancies can operate from free zones without issue if their clients are comfortable with it. Fit-out contractors doing physical works on the mainland need a mainland structure or a mainland branch.

## 4 · First three customers

**Approach fit-out project managers at developer show suites.** Developers completing new residential or mixed-use projects regularly commission fit-outs of their show apartments and sales galleries. These are high-visibility, time-sensitive projects, and the developers and project managers who commission them maintain preferred contractor lists. A personal introduction to a project manager at a developer, backed by a portfolio and references, is the most efficient path to a first commercial project. Attending developer events and trade fairs for the built environment sector is a practical way to generate these introductions.

**Work through architects and real estate agents.** Architects delivering residential or commercial projects require interior designers and fit-out contractors they can recommend to their clients. A relationship with three or four active architects whose project types match your capability can generate referrals consistently. Real estate agents who serve high-value residential clients — the buyer of a newly purchased villa who wants it redesigned before moving in — are a similar referral source for design work.

**Target F&B and retail operators opening new locations.** A restaurant, café, or retail store opening in Dubai is one of the most time-pressured fit-out clients in the market. They have a lease signed, a launch date committed, and a need for a contractor who can deliver. Finding these clients requires monitoring commercial leasing announcements, hospitality trade channels, and the networks of F&B consultants. One successful restaurant fit-out, delivered on time and to budget, typically generates two or three referrals within the same sector.

## 5 · Profit margins & cash cycle

Interior design consultancies earn fees on a time-and-materials basis, a fixed project fee, or a percentage of the total construction cost. Gross margins on design fees are typically high — 50–65% — because the costs are primarily designer salaries and software. Net margin, after overheads, is lower but can reach 25–35% for a well-utilised small team.

Fit-out contracting operates on substantially lower gross margins: typically 20–30%, out of which you must cover site management, materials float, subcontractor coordination, warranties, and rectification costs. Net margins for fit-out contractors often sit at 8–15%, and they depend critically on accurate cost estimation and tight change-order management. One project significantly underpriced or poorly managed can erase several months of profit.

**The cash cycle** is the defining operational challenge of fit-out contracting. Projects are typically billed in milestones: an initial deposit (often 20–30% of the contract value), progress payments tied to agreed construction stages, and a retention payment released after a defect-liability period. In practice, clients are slow to approve milestones, and material and subcontractor costs run ahead of collections. A contractor working on three simultaneous projects may be carrying a working capital deficit of hundreds of thousands of dirhams at any given time. Plan specifically for this. A revolving credit facility or a significant cash reserve is not optional for a fit-out business of any scale.

Breakeven depends entirely on the fee structure and volume. A small design practice breaks even on two to three active projects; a fit-out contractor needs consistent project flow to cover fixed overheads.

## 6 · Top 3 mistakes

**Treating the design licence as sufficient to execute physical works.** Founders who hold an interior design licence and then proceed to manage or execute physical construction — contracting subcontractors, purchasing materials, delivering a completed fit-out — are operating outside their licensed activity. Dubai Municipality inspections on commercial sites check contractor registration, and clients on commercial projects increasingly require documented contractor approvals before allowing site access. The practical consequence of being unregistered is not just a fine; it is a stopped project and a damaged client relationship. If you intend to build, hold the right licence before you start.

**Underestimating project cost and accepting fixed-fee contracts without contingency.** In a market where material prices move, where subcontractors sometimes drop out mid-project, and where clients add scope without adjustment to the fee, a fixed-price contract accepted without a realistic contingency — typically 10–15% of the project cost — is a risk that regularly becomes a loss. Founders eager to win their first contracts accept tight budgets and then discover that the margin, which was always thin, evaporates entirely when anything goes wrong. Price correctly, define scope precisely, and include a written change-order process in every contract.

**Neglecting Civil Defence requirements until the project is complete.** The Civil Defence approval process in Dubai is not something you can retrofit at handover. The MEP design for a commercial space must be approved by Civil Defence before construction begins, and the installed systems must be inspected and certified before the client can use the space. Founders who treat this as a paperwork step to be handled at the end of the project find themselves in a situation where the client cannot occupy a finished — and fully paid-for — space, and the contractor is blamed for the delay. Integrate Civil Defence into your project programme from the first day.

## 7 · Who's already winning

The interior design and fit-out market in Dubai is genuinely competitive at every level. At the top end, European and regional firms with long portfolios in luxury hospitality and high-end residential hold the relationships with the developers and operators who commission those projects. They win on track record, international references, and their ability to manage complex, multi-trade projects at scale.

In the mid-market — the tier that produces the most volume — the winning operators are those who combine design capability with contracting execution under one roof, deliver on time, and communicate consistently through the project. The single biggest complaint from Dubai fit-out clients is not price; it is unreliability. A contractor who answers calls, meets milestones, and owns problems rather than deflecting them builds a referral base faster than any marketing spend.

At the boutique level, designers who have built a visible personal brand — through social media, editorial coverage, or a distinctive aesthetic — command premium fees for residential projects and attract clients who are pre-sold on the designer before the first meeting.

The operators struggling are those who win on price, underdeliver, and depend on finding new clients to replace the ones they have disappointed. The referral network in Dubai's design and construction sector is smaller and faster-moving than it appears.

---

## 8 · Verdict

Interior design and fit-out is a business with real demand, genuine upside, and a regulatory complexity that demands attention from day one. The distinction between design and contracting is not a formality — it is the difference between operating legally and not, and between being able to win commercial contracts and being excluded from them.

This suits the founder who brings genuine design or construction expertise, who understands project management at a practical level, and who has the patience to build a reputation through consistent delivery before pursuing high-value contracts. It also requires more working capital than most founders expect, particularly on the contracting side.

It does not suit the founder who expects to learn the technical side on the client's project, who accepts contracts without understanding the cost, or who treats Civil Defence and Dubai Municipality approvals as optional steps.

**Bottom line:** A properly licenced, technically credible interior design or fit-out business can find a strong position in Dubai's perpetually renewing commercial and residential market — but only if the regulatory and cash-flow foundations are built correctly from the start.
