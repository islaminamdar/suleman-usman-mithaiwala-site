PART TWO — THE FIELD GUIDE  ·  PROFESSIONAL SERVICES

# Chapter 36 · Recruitment & HR Services

*This category has two distinct models with very different capital requirements and regulatory burdens — choosing correctly before you apply for a licence matters more here than almost anywhere else in this guide.*

---

Recruitment and human resources is a sector in which the licensing decision is not a formality but a strategic choice. Operating as a formal recruitment agency — placing workers in exchange for fees — is heavily regulated in the UAE, and for good reason: the history of exploitative recruitment practices globally has prompted authorities to build genuine protections into the framework. Operating as an HR consultancy — advising on people strategy, compensation design, organisational structure, or HR systems — sits in a substantially lighter regulatory environment. Both are legitimate, profitable businesses. The decision about which one to build should be made before you apply for anything.

## 1 · What this business actually is in Dubai

The UAE's workforce is overwhelmingly expatriate, which means every business in the country is, in one form or another, a client of the recruitment and HR sector. Companies need to find people, assess them, hire them, onboard them, manage their visas and contracts, and eventually — whether by resignation, redundancy, or expiry — part with them. Every step in that cycle is a potential service.

A **recruitment agency** (also called a placement agency) actively sources candidates, matches them to employer vacancies, and charges a fee — typically a percentage of the placed candidate's first-year salary, ranging from ten to twenty per cent for professional roles, or a fixed fee for volume hiring. This model requires a MOHRE recruitment-agency licence, which is distinct from a standard trade or professional licence and carries specific conditions including a bank guarantee.

An **HR consultancy** offers advice rather than placement: compensation benchmarking, HR policy development, employee engagement programmes, HR technology implementation, or organisational design. The regulatory burden is lighter — a management or business consultancy licence is typically sufficient — and the bank guarantee requirement that governs recruitment agencies does not apply. The trade-off is that you cannot earn placement fees, which limits the revenue ceiling.

Many operators in this space combine elements of both, using an HR consultancy positioning to lead relationships and handling recruitment mandates through a separately licensed structure or through a strategic alliance with a licensed recruitment partner.

## 2 · Capital required

Capital requirements diverge sharply depending on the model you choose.

**Licence.** A management consultancy or HR consultancy licence via DET or a relevant free zone costs approximately AED 10,000–22,000 for the first year. A MOHRE-registered recruitment agency licence is a separate approval — MOHRE sets the registration fees and conditions, which are subject to change; confirm current figures directly with the authority before planning.

**Bank guarantee for recruitment agencies.** MOHRE requires licensed recruitment agencies to lodge a bank guarantee as a condition of registration. The quantum is set by MOHRE and has historically been substantial — confirm the current requirement, as it represents a significant capital commitment that must be liquid for the duration of your licence. This is the single largest differentiator in capital planning between the two models.

**Premises.** An HR consultancy can operate from a flexi-desk at AED 8,000–15,000 a year. A formal recruitment agency may be required to maintain a physical office; confirm with MOHRE at the time of application.

**Technology.** An applicant tracking system (ATS), a CV database subscription, and LinkedIn Recruiter licences are genuine operational necessities for a recruitment agency; a good ATS with job board integrations costs AED 6,000–15,000 a year. An HR consultancy needs project management and document tools rather than candidate-sourcing infrastructure.

**Staff and visas.** Visa costs run approximately AED 5,000–8,000 per person. Skilled recruiters in Dubai earn AED 8,000–18,000 a month plus commission; HR generalists and consultants are similarly ranged depending on seniority.

**Working capital.** Allow AED 60,000–100,000 beyond setup costs for the HR consultancy model; the recruitment agency model requires more due to the bank guarantee commitment and longer sales cycles.

**HR consultancy lean total:** AED 90,000–130,000. **Recruitment agency lean total:** substantially higher once the bank guarantee is included — confirm the current guarantee requirement with MOHRE and plan accordingly. Comfortable starts in either model require additional runway. All figures are indicative.

## 3 · Licence & jurisdiction

This chapter requires more precision on licensing than most others in Part Two, because the regulatory consequences of getting it wrong are meaningful.

**HR consultancy route.** A management consultancy, human resources consultancy, or HR management activity under a DET professional licence covers advisory services, HR project delivery, training, and organisational consulting. It does not cover placement of workers for a fee. If your model is advisory-only, this is the correct and sufficient licence. Free zone equivalents exist on similar terms.

**Recruitment agency route.** Operating a recruitment or placement agency — earning fees for placing candidates in employment — requires registration with MOHRE under the recruitment agency licensing framework, regardless of where your trade licence is held. MOHRE's rules govern: the fees you may charge candidates (restricted), the protections you must provide, the bank guarantee, and the record-keeping requirements. Non-compliance is a serious regulatory risk, not a technical oversight.

**Practical recommendation.** If you are a solo founder or small team entering this sector for the first time, the HR consultancy model is the faster, lower-capital, and lower-regulatory-risk starting point. Build a track record and a client base. When the business can support the capital commitment and administrative overhead of a MOHRE licence, add it. Many operators have grown from consultancy into agency this way, rather than attempting to carry both from day one.

No additional approvals are required for HR consultancy work beyond the trade licence. For regulated recruitment, engage an employment lawyer to confirm your compliance posture before you begin accepting placement mandates.

## 4 · First three customers

**Your former employers and colleagues.** HR and recruitment are sectors where personal credibility is everything. The head of HR at your previous company, the COO who interviewed you a decade ago, the general manager who watched you place fifty people into their organisation — these are your first calls. Be direct: "I have started an HR consultancy" or "I have launched a recruitment practice" and explain specifically what you do and who you serve. One conversation that leads to a retained mandate is worth a thousand LinkedIn impressions.

**The SME gap in HR.** Most small and medium businesses in Dubai — those with ten to fifty employees — cannot justify a full-time HR manager. They need HR infrastructure (offer letters, employment contracts, policies, MOHRE registration guidance, visa management) but cannot afford a senior employee to deliver it. A fractional HR model — where you serve as the part-time HR function for three or four SME clients simultaneously — provides you with retainer income and your clients with access to professional HR that they could not otherwise afford. This is the easiest first-client model for an HR consultancy.

**Industry-specific recruitment.** A recruiter who specialises deeply in one sector — financial services, technology, hospitality, or construction — will be sought out by employers in that sector who are frustrated with generalists who do not understand the role or the market. If you spent ten years in banking, your network is in banking, and your first three recruitment mandates will almost certainly come from that world. Lead with the sector knowledge, not the recruitment methodology.

## 5 · Profit margins & cash cycle

The two models have meaningfully different financial profiles.

**HR consultancy.** Monthly retainers for fractional HR services to SMEs run AED 5,000–15,000 per client, depending on scope and the size of the business. Project work — designing a compensation framework, implementing an HR information system, running a restructuring — is priced as fixed-fee engagements at AED 15,000–60,000 or more depending on complexity. Gross margins on consultancy work are typically 55–70% for a founder-led practice where the primary input is time. Cash cycle is straightforward: retainers paid monthly, project fees with a deposit and milestone structure.

**Recruitment agency.** Placement fees for professional and managerial roles are typically 12–18% of the placed candidate's first-year base salary. At AED 15,000 a month (a common mid-management salary in Dubai at the time of writing), a successful placement earns approximately AED 21,600–32,400 per hire. Volume hiring for frontline roles uses fixed fees per placement, typically AED 3,000–8,000. The cash cycle for recruitment is lumpy: you carry the cost of sourcing, screening, and presenting candidates before any fee is earned, and the fee is earned only on successful placement. A deal that takes three months to close and then falls through during probation creates a clawback obligation under most fee agreements. Gross margin on placed fees is high — 60% or more for a small team — but the revenue is irregular and probation-period clawbacks are a genuine cash risk.

**Breakeven** for an HR consultancy with AED 30,000–40,000 in monthly costs requires three to five retainer clients — achievable in months nine to fourteen. A recruitment agency has higher fixed costs and an irregular revenue pattern; breakeven depends heavily on placement frequency and average fee.

## 6 · Top 3 mistakes

**Launching a recruitment agency without fully understanding the MOHRE framework.** The regulations governing recruitment agencies in the UAE are not complex to read but are consequential to ignore. Fee structures that are standard in some markets — charging the candidate a placement fee, for instance — are restricted or prohibited under the UAE framework. Operators who adopt practices from other markets without confirming their legality here create compliance risk that MOHRE will eventually find. Read the regulations in full, engage an employment lawyer for a compliance review, and build your fee structures and contracts around what is actually permitted.

**Positioning as a generalist recruiter.** "We recruit across all industries and all levels" is not a positioning; it is an absence of one. In Dubai's crowded recruitment market — where multinational agencies, regional specialists, and hundreds of boutique firms compete — a generalist with no track record in any specific sector is indistinguishable from its competitors. Clients hire recruiters they trust to understand the role, the market rate, and the candidate pool. That trust is built through demonstrated sector knowledge. Choose a sector and work it until you are genuinely known in it.

**Treating candidate relationships as transactional.** A common failure mode in recruitment is to focus entirely on the employer client — the one who pays the fee — and treat candidates as a resource rather than a relationship. In Dubai, where professional networks are dense and reputations travel quickly, a candidate who felt dismissed, poorly briefed, or misled will tell a dozen colleagues. The placed candidate of today is the hiring manager of five years from now. Treat every interaction with every candidate as a long-term relationship investment, not a step in a transaction.

## 7 · Who's already winning

The operators building sustainable businesses in this sector tend to share one characteristic: depth over breadth.

The boutique specialist that serves technology companies — understanding the specific roles, the compensation expectations of engineers and product managers, the visa complexities for certain nationalities, and the cultural fit dynamics of a fast-growing tech team — is worth more to that client than a large generalist agency that also has a financial services desk and a construction practice. The specialist can speak authoritatively; the generalist can only follow a brief.

On the HR consultancy side, the firms winning consistently are those that have built a fractional model — several clients paying retainers for ongoing access to a senior HR professional — rather than chasing one-off projects. The retainer base provides stability that project work alone cannot, and it deepens the client relationship over time, generating both repeat project mandates and introductions to the client's network.

Both archetypes operate with small, senior teams and high utilisation. They do not try to compete on headcount or office size. They win on quality of relationships, quality of delivery, and a reputation that makes the phone ring before the pitch document goes out.

## 8 · Verdict

Recruitment and HR services is a genuinely attractive category for the right founder — one with relevant sector experience, an existing professional network, and the temperament to build a relationship-led practice over time. The HR consultancy route is the more accessible starting point: lower capital, lighter regulation, and faster to first revenue. The recruitment agency route carries a meaningful capital commitment in the bank guarantee and a more demanding regulatory framework, but the per-placement fees can make the economics compelling once volume is established.

Neither model rewards impatience. Both require a founder who is known for something specific before they open the doors.

**Bottom line:** Enter as an HR consultancy if you are building from scratch; add the MOHRE recruitment licence only when the capital, the compliance infrastructure, and the placement volume justify it.
