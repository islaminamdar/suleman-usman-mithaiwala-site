PART TWO — THE FIELD GUIDE  ·  FOOD & BEVERAGE

# Chapter 9 · Catering & Private Chef

*Event-driven demand, corporate contracts, and a different relationship with rent — this is F&B with a more manageable cost structure.*

---

Catering is the food business that does not require you to fill seats every night. Your revenue comes in peaks — weddings, corporate conferences, Ramadan gatherings, Eid celebrations — and the discipline is in managing those peaks profitably and using quieter periods to build the client pipeline for the next one.

## 1 · What this business actually is in Dubai

Catering in Dubai covers a wide range of operations. At one end is the private chef: a single culinary professional working in a client's home for a dinner party, a recurring weekly service, or a holiday residency. At the other end is a full event catering company capable of feeding 800 guests at a corporate gala, managing the service team, the rental equipment, and the logistics across multiple venues.

Between those two extremes sit several commercially viable formats. Office catering — supplying daily lunches to a corporate client on a standing contract — provides the kind of predictable, recurring revenue that a wedding-only caterer never sees. Event catering for smaller private gatherings — birthday dinners, family celebrations, villa parties — is lower in revenue per event but higher in frequency, especially in a city where home entertaining is common among senior expats and Emirati families. Ramadan and Eid catering is a seasonal peak that well-established caterers plan around all year, and that a new entrant can use to build volume and references quickly.

What makes catering structurally different from a restaurant or café is the relationship with fixed costs. There is no dining room lease to pay every month regardless of covers. The kitchen can be a licensed commissary facility, a shared commercial space, or — for smaller operations — a purpose-built kitchen in an industrial unit. Staff can, within legal limits, be scaled to event volume. The business does not need to find 80 customers every evening to cover its costs; it needs to find ten events per month, price them correctly, and execute them well.

The trade-off is revenue volatility. A month with three weddings and four corporate events can be excellent; a month with two small private bookings is thin. Cash flow management, and the discipline to price events at full cost — not at the rate needed to win the booking — is the core operating skill in this business.

## 2 · Capital required

The figures below are indicative at the time of writing; verify current rates and consult Part Three.

**Trade licence (DET, mainland):** AED 12,000–18,000 per year. The activity must specifically cover catering services; if you also intend to provide private chef services to individuals in their homes, this should be noted in the activity and confirmed with DET.

**Dubai Municipality food permit:** AED 2,000–5,000, tied to the kitchen address. A catering business preparing food offsite requires a permitted production kitchen; you cannot legally prepare food in an unlicensed domestic kitchen for commercial sale.

**Kitchen facility:** If leasing an industrial or commercial kitchen unit, expect AED 3,000–10,000 per month depending on size and location. Some catering companies lease shared commissary space by the hour or day, reducing the fixed monthly cost but constraining availability. Fit-out of a dedicated catering kitchen — commercial extraction, refrigeration, stainless surfaces — runs AED 60,000–150,000. Civil Defence approval is required.

**Equipment:** Chafing dishes, serving equipment, transport containers, and field equipment for service are a significant and often underestimated line: AED 30,000–80,000 to equip a business capable of serving 100-plus covers at an event.

**Vehicles:** A refrigerated transport van is effectively mandatory for a catering operation of any scale: AED 80,000–140,000 to purchase, or AED 3,000–5,500 per month to lease.

**Staff and visas:** Core kitchen staff plus the ability to scale service staff per event. A lean permanent team of three to five runs AED 15,000–30,000 per month; casual event staff represent an additional variable cost.

**Marketing and trade presence:** AED 5,000–15,000 for a professional portfolio, website, and initial corporate outreach.

**Working capital:** Three to four months of fixed costs, plus the float to purchase ingredients ahead of events that may not settle for 30 days.

A realistic first-year all-in budget for a properly set up catering business runs AED 350,000–600,000.

## 3 · Licence & jurisdiction

A catering business requires a DET trade licence with catering activity, and a Dubai Municipality food establishment permit for the production kitchen. The kitchen must be approved as a food production facility; this is a location-specific permit that the Municipality can inspect without notice.

Food handlers — every person who prepares or touches food in a commercial setting — must hold a valid DHA occupational health card. At least one Person-in-Charge food safety certificate holder must be on site during production.

For events at venues — hotels, conference centres, event spaces — the venue itself is typically licensed for food service, and the catering company operates within the venue's licence. For private residences and outdoor locations, the catering company's own licence and permits cover the operation. If you are planning to cater at public events or in areas that require special event permits, those permits are the responsibility of the event organiser, but the caterer must confirm they are in place before arriving.

A private chef working exclusively in clients' homes operates under the catering activity on a DET licence. The food preparation happens in the client's kitchen, which is not a licensed commercial facility — confirm with the Municipality whether and how this is regulated, as the position may evolve.

The jurisdiction recommendation is straightforward: mainland DET, with the kitchen facility on the mainland. Free zone structures serve no practical purpose for a catering business whose customers are across the city, and they complicate the Municipality permit process.

## 4 · First three customers

The first three clients for a catering business come from relationships, not from search engines. Catering is a high-trust purchase: a corporate event manager or a family planning a wedding is not browsing a delivery app. They are asking someone they already trust for a recommendation.

**Direct outreach to corporate office managers.** Every company in Dubai's central business districts has someone responsible for office events, staff lunches, and corporate entertaining. That person — often an executive assistant, office manager, or HR manager — receives a stream of catering enquiries and has typically settled on two or three preferred suppliers. To become one of them, you must contact them before an event is on the calendar, not after. A brief, professional introduction — with a menu, a price guide, and a portfolio of previous work — sent to 30 to 50 office contacts in your first month will generate two to four responses, which is all you need.

**Event planners and wedding coordinators.** Dubai has a substantial independent event planning industry. These professionals are not caterers; they are orchestrators who assemble suppliers for each event. A caterer who is known, reliable, and easy to work with appears on their recommended lists and receives referrals for every event that planner manages. Introduce yourself to five to ten event planners in the first month, offer a tasting, and make it clear what you do better than generalist competitors.

**Ramadan and Eid as a launch window.** If your business launches in the two months before Ramadan, the Iftar catering market provides a concentrated period of high demand that generates both revenue and references. A single well-executed Iftar for 50 people produces social content, a client relationship, and the kind of visible volume that other potential clients notice.

## 5 · Profit margins & cash cycle

Gross margins in catering — before staffing and logistics — typically run 55–70% on food cost. Labour and transport are the variables that determine whether the net margin is healthy or thin. A well-priced private dining event for 12 guests, where the chef provides both cooking and service, can achieve a net margin of 25–35% per event. A large corporate event requiring significant event staff and equipment rental may net 10–18%.

The cash cycle presents a specific challenge. Catering jobs are typically quoted in advance, with a deposit — commonly 30–50% of the event value — paid on booking. The balance is due on or shortly after the event. That structure sounds favourable, but it has two traps. First, the deposit does not always cover the ingredient purchasing and staff scheduling costs that occur before the event. Second, corporate clients in particular may request 30-day payment terms on the balance, creating a gap between delivery and final settlement that must be financed from working capital.

The seasonal nature of corporate and event catering in Dubai means that the first and fourth quarters of the calendar year are typically the strongest — corporate events cluster around Q1 strategic conferences and Q4 year-end functions, while Ramadan and Eid create significant demand in whichever calendar quarter they fall. A catering business that does not build its pipeline for each peak during the preceding quiet period will be reactive and under-resourced when the volume arrives.

## 6 · Top 3 mistakes

**Underpricing to win the first contracts.** The temptation to price below cost to build a portfolio and generate references is understandable and, in practice, damaging. A catering business that wins ten events at a loss has a portfolio of testimonials and an empty bank account. Price every event at full cost — ingredients, labour, transport, equipment amortisation, and overhead allocation — from the first contract. Offer value through the quality of the experience and the reliability of the service, not through a price you cannot sustain.

**Failing to account for all event costs.** Catering quotes that exclude or underestimate event staff, equipment rental, transport, and post-event cleaning routinely result in the event being profitable on paper and loss-making in practice. Build a job costing template before you quote for anything, and use it without exception. Every variable — the number of service staff hours, the cost of each piece of hired equipment, the fuel and driver time for the transport vehicle — must appear on the template before the quote goes out.

**Neglecting corporate relationships after the first event.** A corporate client who was impressed by their first event with you will not necessarily call you for the next one. They are busy; they have a roster of suppliers; they will use whoever is at the top of their mind when the next event comes around. A brief follow-up note after every event, a reminder call six weeks before their typical event cycle, and an annual relationship-maintenance visit — even just dropping in a sample menu — are the activities that convert a one-time buyer into a standing account. The catering business with five corporate accounts generating three to four events per year each has a fundamentally more stable financial base than one chasing individual bookings.

## 7 · Who's already winning

The catering operators with consistent pipeline in Dubai are doing three things that differentiates them from the wider field.

They are specialists, not generalists. A caterer who is known for a specific cuisine — Lebanese, South Asian, Mediterranean — or for a specific event type — corporate conferences, private villa dinners, Ramadan Iftars — is easier to recommend and easier to remember than a caterer who does everything. Specialisation makes your marketing more targeted, your kitchen more efficient, and your referrals more reliable.

They have invested in presentation before operations. In a market where the buying decision is almost always made based on a portfolio, a tasting, or a personal recommendation, the catering company with professional food photography, a clean and current website, and a well-designed proposal template wins enquiries that its equally capable but less polished competitor does not see.

They manage their event calendar with the same seriousness as their kitchen. The operators who grow in this category treat their booking pipeline as a financial instrument — they know their breakeven event volume per month, they know which months are historically quiet, and they are marketing and selling in those quiet months for the busy ones ahead.

## 8 · Verdict

Catering and private chef services offer a more manageable fixed-cost structure than a restaurant or café, with genuine upside in corporate and event demand — a category that Dubai's economy, its conference calendar, its wedding culture, and its Ramadan season sustain at meaningful volume. The business is relationship-driven and operationally demanding on event days, but the absence of a full-time dining room removes the daily operational grind of a seated food business.

Who should proceed: an experienced chef or hospitality professional with a specific niche, AED 350,000 or more in capital, and a realistic plan for the first ten corporate or private clients.

Who should pause: anyone who expects catering to be lower-pressure than a restaurant simply because it is event-based. Well-executed events are logistically complex, and a single failure at scale is reputationally costly.

**Bottom line:** Catering rewards relationships, specialisation, and discipline in pricing — the operators who get those three right find Dubai's event market genuinely substantial.
