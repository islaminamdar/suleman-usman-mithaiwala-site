PART TWO — THE FIELD GUIDE  ·  MOBILITY & TRANSPORT

# Chapter 24 · Delivery & Last-Mile Logistics

*Margins here are thin by design — the operators who survive are the ones who treat operational discipline as their only competitive advantage.*

---

Last-mile delivery in Dubai is a real industry with real demand, and it is one of the hardest businesses in this book to run profitably. The barriers to entry are low: you need riders, vehicles, and a client. The barriers to survival are much higher: you need to manage a rider workforce across multiple visa and HR obligations, keep vehicles compliant with RTA requirements, price accurately enough to cover costs that are constantly in motion, and do all of this while competing against established aggregators who have already built the technology infrastructure and the client base. This chapter is not a discouragement. It is a precise description of what the business actually requires.

## 1 · What this business actually is in Dubai

Last-mile delivery in Dubai encompasses several distinct operating models. The most visible is the aggregator-partner model: an operator provides riders and vehicles under contract to a platform aggregator — a restaurant delivery platform, a grocery delivery service, or a quick-commerce operator — which provides the technology, the client base, and the order flow. The operator manages the workforce and the vehicles; the aggregator manages the customer relationship and the booking.

The second model is direct e-commerce delivery: an operator contracts with online retailers, fashion brands, electronics sellers, or specialty food businesses to fulfil their own-brand deliveries — the operator's riders arrive with the retailer's branded packaging, and the customer experience belongs to the retailer. This model typically carries higher per-delivery revenue than aggregator work and allows for more direct relationship building.

The third model is courier and document delivery, primarily B2B: law firms, accounting practices, banks, and government-facing businesses that need documents and items moved quickly across the city. This is a smaller segment but commands higher per-delivery fees and carries less operational complexity than food or same-day e-commerce.

Dubai's geography is favourable for last-mile operations. The city is dense in its commercial and residential clusters, and the concentration of demand in areas like Downtown, JLT, Silicon Oasis, and the villa communities of Arabian Ranches and JBR means that well-designed routes can achieve high delivery density — the critical driver of per-rider productivity.

The customer base is predominantly the businesses that need deliveries fulfilled, not the end recipients. Your selling is B2B — acquiring clients who will generate consistent, recurring volume — rather than consumer marketing.

## 2 · Capital required

Last-mile delivery is more labour-intensive and less capital-intensive than other businesses in this category, but the cumulative cost of a rider-and-vehicle workforce is substantial.

**Licence and RTA permits.** A DET commercial licence for transport and delivery services, combined with any RTA vehicle permits required for your fleet type, runs approximately AED 15,000–25,000 at the time of writing. Confirm current figures with DET and the RTA directly; these are subject to change. If operating motorcycles commercially, the vehicle registration category must reflect commercial use.

**Vehicles.** The typical delivery fleet consists of motorcycles (for food and small-parcel delivery) and light commercial vans (for larger e-commerce, furniture, and document delivery). A motorcycle suitable for commercial delivery can be purchased in the range of AED 8,000–18,000 at the time of writing; a light van or pickup in the AED 70,000–120,000 range. A lean ten-rider motorcycle operation requires AED 80,000–180,000 in vehicle capital. Vans add significantly. Leasing rather than purchasing reduces upfront capital but increases the ongoing fixed cost.

**Rider visas and sponsorship.** This is the most significant recurring cost line in the business, and the one most frequently underestimated. Each rider requires a UAE employment visa, Emirates ID, MOHRE registration, and medical. Budget AED 8,000–15,000 per rider in the first year for these costs. The employer — your company — is the sponsor, which means visa, health insurance, and end-of-service gratuity obligations attach to you. For a ten-rider operation, this is AED 80,000–150,000 in first-year HR capital, before salaries.

**Salaries.** Delivery rider salaries typically run AED 1,800–3,500 per month at the time of writing, plus fuel allowance or fuel cost depending on contract structure. A ten-rider team costs AED 18,000–35,000 per month in gross salaries before employer ancillaries.

**Insurance.** Comprehensive commercial vehicle insurance for motorcycles and vans, plus employer liability and workmen's compensation. Budget AED 1,500–4,000 per vehicle per year at the time of writing; confirm with insurers.

**Technology and operations.** A dispatch system, route optimisation tool, and client reporting capability are not optional for a professionally run operation. Basic SaaS tools are available from AED 500–2,000 per month; custom platforms are substantially more.

**Working capital.** Four to six months of salary, fuel, and vehicle costs while client contracts stabilise. Budget AED 150,000–250,000 for a ten-rider operation.

A realistic ten-rider launch with motorcycles, proper visa compliance, and basic technology sits at AED 450,000–750,000 in first-year total investment. Verify current figures and consult Part Three.

## 3 · Licence & jurisdiction

The regulatory picture for last-mile delivery involves multiple layers, and getting all of them right simultaneously is one of the operational challenges of the early months.

The DET mainland commercial licence for transport and delivery is the starting point. This is a standard commercial activity registration, and physical premises — a warehouse, dispatch office, or operational base — are required. The address on the licence must correspond to a verifiable operational location; a flexi-desk will not satisfy DET for this activity category.

The RTA layer covers the vehicles. Motorcycles and commercial vehicles used for commercial delivery must be registered under a commercial activity licence, not as private vehicles. If your riders use personal motorcycles — a common cost-cutting measure that does not hold up to scrutiny — those vehicles are not commercially registered, and the insurance cover on a personal vehicle does not extend to commercial use. An accident during a commercial delivery on a personally registered bike is an uninsured event. This is a compliance issue and a financial exposure that has ended small delivery operations. Use commercially registered vehicles from day one.

MOHRE governs the employment of riders on the mainland. Riders must be employed under UAE labour law: contracts in Arabic, MOHRE registration, health insurance, and end-of-service gratuity obligations. Using self-employed or freelance rider arrangements is not straightforward under UAE mainland labour law; confirm the permissible structures with a legal adviser before assuming a gig-economy model is available to you.

Free zone structures are not practical for this business. Operations, riders, and vehicles are all on the mainland; the client base is on the mainland. A mainland DET licence is the correct structure.

## 4 · First three customers

The first three clients in last-mile delivery come from direct sales, not from inbound enquiries.

**Restaurant groups and cloud kitchens not yet on aggregators.** A number of smaller restaurant operations and cloud kitchens in Dubai serve their own customer base through their own ordering system and need a reliable delivery partner rather than a platform. These businesses pay higher per-delivery rates than aggregator contracts because you are absorbing what would otherwise be an aggregator commission. Identify five to ten such operations in your target geography, approach the owner or operations manager directly, and offer a trial period at competitive rates with a clear service level agreement. Two converted clients generate consistent daily volume.

**E-commerce businesses seeking own-brand delivery.** Online fashion boutiques, specialty food brands, and consumer goods sellers in Dubai increasingly want their delivery experience to be branded — their packaging, their uniforms, their timing window — rather than a generic aggregator experience. These clients pay a premium for that control. Approach the operations or fulfilment managers of five to ten online sellers with delivery volume in your target geography. A reliable, professional service with real-time tracking and accurate ETAs is your differentiator.

**B2B document and courier clients.** Law firms, audit practices, real estate agencies, and banks in the DIFC and along Sheikh Zayed Road have ongoing document delivery needs and value speed and reliability above price. Approach operations managers and office managers directly. A monthly retainer for guaranteed response-time delivery is more valuable to these clients than an ad hoc per-delivery rate, and more valuable to you for cash-flow predictability.

## 5 · Profit margins & cash cycle

Last-mile delivery is a low-margin business. That is not a criticism; it is the structural reality of the industry globally, and Dubai is no exception. The business model is viable, but only for operators who understand exactly where the margin lives and protect it systematically.

**Revenue per delivery.** Aggregator contracts typically pay AED 8–18 per delivery at the time of writing, depending on distance, vehicle type, and volume commitments. Direct e-commerce contracts pay AED 15–35 per delivery. B2B courier rates vary more widely — AED 25–80 per job depending on urgency and distance. Confirm current market rates, as these shift with competitive dynamics and fuel costs.

**Cost per delivery.** A rider on AED 2,500 per month completing forty deliveries per day across twenty-five working days delivers approximately 1,000 deliveries per month. Rider cost per delivery: AED 2.50. Add fuel (AED 1.50–3.00 per delivery depending on distance and vehicle type), vehicle depreciation and maintenance allocation (AED 0.80–1.50), insurance allocation (AED 0.30–0.60), and a share of management overhead. Total cost per delivery for a productive rider: AED 6–9.

**Gross margins** on aggregator contracts at AED 10–15 per delivery, with a cost of AED 6–9, are AED 4–8 — a gross margin of twenty-five to fifty per cent on paper. The problem is that riders are rarely at one hundred per cent productivity; idle time between orders, route inefficiency, and peak-off-peak imbalance reduce effective deliveries per rider per day and push cost per delivery up. At sixty per cent rider productivity, the economics look very different.

**Net margins** for a well-run operation in this segment run five to twelve per cent. That is not a typo. Operators who treat this as a high-margin business are not modelling fuel, downtime, visa renewal costs, and vehicle maintenance honestly.

**The cash cycle** is relatively short on the revenue side — aggregator platforms typically settle weekly or bi-weekly; direct clients are invoiced monthly. The stress point is the continuous outflow: salaries are monthly, fuel is daily, visa renewals cluster. Cash management requires weekly attention, not monthly.

Realistic breakeven: ten to eighteen months, assuming consistent client volume from month three or four.

## 6 · Top 3 mistakes

**Sponsoring riders before securing the client volume.** The sequence matters enormously. Each rider visa is a commitment: the employment contract, the MOHRE registration, the health insurance obligation, and the end-of-service gratuity liability all attach from day one. Operators who hire ten riders to be ready for expected volume, then discover the client contracts take three months longer to close than anticipated, are paying ten salaries, ten insurance policies, and all the associated HR costs with no revenue to offset them. Build the client base first — even with a small initial team — and add riders as volume justifies it.

**Competing on price with aggregators on their own terms.** The large delivery aggregators have technology, volume, and network effects that a new entrant cannot replicate. Attempting to win business by quoting below aggregator rates is a race to the bottom that a new operator cannot win — the aggregator will simply adjust its commercial terms with its existing clients. The correct strategy is to compete where aggregators are weak: personalised service, branded delivery experience, flexible scheduling, or specialist handling (temperature-controlled, fragile, high-value). Own a niche; do not try to replicate the aggregator.

**Under-investing in route optimisation.** Rider productivity — deliveries per hour, deliveries per day — is the single biggest lever on unit economics. An operator running manual WhatsApp-based dispatch, with no route planning tool, consistently under-utilises riders by twenty to thirty per cent compared with operators using even a basic dispatch and routing system. The cost of a routing tool — AED 500–2,000 per month — is recovered in the first week of improved rider productivity. Operating without one is a false economy that compounds every month.

## 7 · Who's already winning

The operators winning consistently in Dubai's last-mile market are not the ones with the largest rider pools. They are the ones who have solved the unit economics problem at the rider level and then scaled those economics across a focused client base.

The most effective independent model is the specialist operator: a business that serves a specific sector — restaurant groups, fashion e-commerce, pharmaceutical delivery, or corporate document courier — with a service design built around that sector's specific needs. These operators charge appropriately for the specialisation, maintain high rider productivity through focused routing, and build client relationships that are resistant to platform switching. Their retention rate is high because their clients value the relationship as much as the price.

A second group has built a hybrid model: aggregator contract volume provides the floor of rider utilisation — the baseline that covers fixed costs — while direct B2B clients provide the margin above that floor. This structure requires the operational sophistication to manage two different service standards simultaneously, but it is the most economically resilient model in the category.

What the winners share is an absolute focus on the cost-per-delivery number. They know it by rider, by client, by day of week, and by vehicle type. They manage it actively, not passively. The operators who do not know their cost per delivery do not survive long enough to find out.

## 8 · Verdict

Last-mile delivery suits a founder who has a specific client committed before riders are sponsored, who understands that the business lives in the cost-per-delivery number rather than the gross revenue line, and who has the operational temperament to manage a large, distributed workforce across visa, vehicle, and client obligations simultaneously. It is operationally demanding in a way that many other businesses in this book are not.

It does not suit the founder who enters expecting the demand to be obvious or the margins to be generous. Neither is true. The opportunity is real — Dubai's e-commerce growth and restaurant delivery culture are durable — but it rewards operators who respect the complexity of the model, not those who underestimate it.

The founders who survive the first two years in this business have typically built something the category rarely produces: a delivery operation that clients trust enough to stay with even when a cheaper alternative arrives.

**Bottom line:** Last-mile delivery in Dubai is a thin-margin, high-discipline business — the only path to profitability is knowing your cost per delivery precisely, securing client volume before sponsoring riders, and owning a niche where your service justifies the price.
