PART TWO — THE FIELD GUIDE  ·  TOURISM & EVENTS

# Chapter 31 · Event Management & Wedding Planning

*A business that stages other people's most important moments — and runs on deposits, deadlines, and a reputation cycle that forgives nothing.*

---

Dubai's event economy operates at unusual scale. The city hosts international exhibitions, government ceremonies, corporate summits, and, at the other end of the spectrum, the most elaborately produced private weddings in the world. The event management company and the wedding planner differ in tone but are identical in mechanics. Both take a client's vision, a supplier network, and a budget, and deliver a result that cannot be redone if it goes wrong on the day. There is no second take.

## 1 · What this business actually is in Dubai

An event management company in Dubai arranges, coordinates, and produces events for corporate or private clients. On the corporate side this means product launches, conferences, exhibitions, team-building programmes, gala dinners, and MICE events (meetings, incentives, conferences, and exhibitions). On the private side the dominant product is weddings — Dubai attracts high-budget destination weddings from South Asia, the Arab world, the UK, and Africa, and the local expatriate community generates its own steady demand.

The two models are structurally similar. You receive a brief and a budget. You engage a supplier network — venues, caterers, audio-visual production companies, florists, photographers, entertainment acts, and logistics providers. You project-manage the production and deliver the event. Your margin sits between what the client pays and what the suppliers charge, plus your management fee. In practice, most event companies charge a combination of management fee and a percentage mark-up on supplier costs.

What makes this business distinctively Dubai is the concentration of production scale. A mid-range wedding in Dubai can have a production budget of AED 300,000–1,500,000. A corporate gala might require a venue deposit alone of AED 50,000–200,000. You will be asked to commit to venues and suppliers before the client's full deposit has cleared. Managing the cash sequencing of those commitments is the central skill of this business. Everything else is craft. This is survival.

## 2 · Capital required

Event management is a moderate-capital business. You are not holding inventory or operating plant, but you need professional infrastructure, a visible track record, and enough working capital to bridge the gap between client deposits and supplier payment schedules.

**Licence and approvals.** A DET event management licence (or equivalent creative services activity on the mainland) is the base requirement. At the time of writing, expect AED 12,000–25,000 for the licence. Event-specific permits — from DET, Dubai Municipality, and in the case of public events or those with significant crowd management requirements, from the police — are separate and project-specific costs. Budget AED 5,000–20,000 per major event for permits, depending on scale and venue. Confirm current figures with the relevant authorities.

**Premises.** A professional office is expected by corporate clients and helps establish credibility. Allow AED 60,000–120,000 annually for a modest but presentable space.

**Staff and visas.** A senior event producer, a client manager, and a production coordinator at launch. Allow AED 150,000–250,000 annually in combined salaries and AED 8,000–15,000 per person in visa and Emirates ID costs at current rates. Freelance production staff are a major variable cost, engaged per event.

**Equipment.** Basic décor, linen, and props if you intend to own rather than hire every element: AED 40,000–100,000. Many early-stage companies hire everything and build ownership selectively.

**Marketing and portfolio development.** Photography of your first events, a professional website, and social media development: AED 20,000–50,000.

**Working capital.** The essential item: enough to bridge client deposit receipt and supplier payment commitments across two or three simultaneous events: AED 100,000–200,000.

A lean start: AED 300,000–500,000. A comfortable, professionally equipped launch: AED 500,000–900,000. All figures are indicative; verify before committing.

## 3 · Licence & jurisdiction

The base licence is a **DET commercial licence** with an event management or event planning activity. On the mainland, this typically falls under a general professional or commercial licence category; confirm the specific activity code with DET. DTCM does not regulate event management companies as such — the tourism authority's remit here is primarily hotels, tour operators, and travel agents — but events held in Dubai may require permits through DTCM if they involve tourism-related venues or international visitor programming.

**Event-specific permits are not included in the base licence.** Public events — those held in outdoor spaces, public venues, or with attendances above certain thresholds — require approval from **Dubai Municipality** (for health and safety compliance, food handling, and venue standards) and, for events with significant public presence or entertainment, from the **Dubai Police**. The permit schedule varies by event type, size, and venue. Build permit timelines into your project planning from the outset — approvals can take two to four weeks and must be secured before supplier commitments are made.

**Mainland is the correct jurisdiction** for an event management business serving local corporate and private clients. Free zone companies can operate event management activities but may encounter restrictions on in-country commercial operations with mainland clients; take specific advice before choosing.

Register for VAT with the FTA once you cross the threshold. Event management services are standard-rated at 5%; the treatment of recharges for third-party supplier costs requires specific advice — do not assume all recharges are automatically subject to or exempt from VAT.

## 4 · First three customers

**Corporate pilot events.** The most reliable path to the first paying client is the small-scale corporate event: a product presentation, a team iftar dinner, a company anniversary celebration. These are lower stakes for the client than a major conference, which means you can be considered before you have the portfolio a major event requires. Reach corporate buyers through LinkedIn, direct outreach to marketing managers and executive assistants at active companies, and through business networking groups — the Dubai Chamber, industry associations, and professional networks are all productive. One delivered event, well documented, becomes the evidence that opens the next door.

**Venue partnerships.** Hotels, private clubs, and venue spaces have their own event enquiry pipelines and frequently recommend preferred event companies. Visit the events or banqueting managers at the venues you want to work with, introduce yourself, and leave materials. A venue that trusts you will refer clients who arrive venue-first and need a planner — and those clients are already committed to spending.

**Bridal networks and wedding shows.** For wedding planners specifically, the first three clients often come through bridal shows such as the Wedding Festival Dubai, through recommendations from venues that host weddings, and through the South Asian and Arab community networks that generate the highest-value wedding enquiries. Instagram is genuinely important here — a wedding planner who cannot show a visually compelling portfolio will not win enquiries in a market where the visual product is everything.

## 5 · Profit margins & cash cycle

Gross margins in event management typically run at 20–35% of the total event value, depending on the structure of the fee. A management fee-only model (client pays suppliers directly) is lower risk but earns a flat fee of AED 15,000–80,000 per event depending on scale. A full-service model — where you collect the full event budget, pay suppliers, and retain the margin — can earn 25–35% gross but puts the cash flow and supplier risk entirely on you.

The cash cycle in this business is a sequencing exercise, not a collection exercise. Clients generally pay a deposit of 30–50% on engagement and the remainder in stages before the event. Suppliers, however, often require their own deposits — venues, in particular, expect significant advance commitments. The gap between receiving the client deposit and owing the supplier deposit is where this business creates cash pressure. A project timeline with fifty simultaneous commitments and only one client deposit received is not a cash-flow problem in theory. It is a crisis on the ground, and it arrives on a schedule you set yourself.

Net margins, after fixed office costs and staff, settle at 10–20% for a well-run company. The business is lumpy by nature: three excellent events in a quarter and then a slow period. Time to breakeven: twelve to twenty months, depending on how quickly corporate accounts are secured.

## 6 · Top 3 mistakes

**Spending the client deposit before the suppliers are paid.** This is the most common cash failure in event management. The deposit arrives, the bank balance looks healthy, and the company covers its own fixed costs or invests in marketing — only to discover that the venue deposit is due in two weeks and the money has already gone. The professional discipline is simple but non-negotiable: ring-fence client deposits. The money is not yours until the event is delivered and final accounts are settled. Keep it separate, draw on it only for project expenses, and reconcile after every event.

**Underestimating the permit timeline.** Dubai's permit system for events is well-defined, but it is not fast. A founder who does not build permit lead times into the project schedule — and does not account for the cost of permits in the client proposal — will face last-minute scrambles and cost overruns. Every proposal should include a permit timeline as a project milestone, and the permit costs should be passed through to the client (with a small management margin) rather than absorbed.

**Acquiring clients by winning on price.** Weddings and corporate events are not commodity services. A client who engages you because you were the cheapest option will hold you to a standard the budget cannot support, will request changes that erode your margin, and will not refer you to anyone when it is over. The clients worth winning are those who choose you for what you produce, not what you charge. Build a portfolio that demonstrates quality at a specific level, and price to it. Competing on price in this market is a slow path to a high-stress, low-margin business.

## 7 · Who's already winning

The event companies building durable businesses in Dubai have two consistent characteristics. First, they have a defined specialism — corporate production, South Asian weddings, government events, luxury private dining — and they are known for it within a specific client community. The generalists compete on every enquiry; the specialists attract enquiries from clients who have already decided they want exactly what that company does.

Second, the winning companies manage the production calendar, not just the production itself. They have systems: a supplier database that is maintained, a permit checklist that is rehearsed, a client briefing process that surfaces scope-creep early. Events are complex logistical exercises, and the operators who treat them as such — rather than as creative exercises loosely organised — consistently outperform. Reputation in this market travels fast in both directions; a single poorly executed event discussed in the right community networks will cost more future revenue than any marketing budget can recover.

## 8 · Verdict

Event management and wedding planning is a business with real upside — strong gross margins on individual projects, a high-spending client base, and a market that is genuinely growing as Dubai's corporate and private event calendar expands. It is also one of the highest-pressure service businesses in this book. There is no recovering from a failed event on the night; the product cannot be reworked after delivery.

It suits a founder who has event production experience, strong supplier relationships already in place, and the organisational discipline to manage multiple moving parts under deadline pressure. It does not suit a founder who likes events as a consumer — that is a different skill set entirely.

**Bottom line:** high-reward for the experienced, project-disciplined operator; genuinely risky for anyone building their production skills on a client's AED 800,000 wedding budget.
