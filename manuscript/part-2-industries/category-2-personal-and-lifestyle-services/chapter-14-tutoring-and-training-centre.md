PART TWO — THE FIELD GUIDE  ·  PERSONAL & LIFESTYLE SERVICES

# Chapter 14 · Tutoring & Training Centre

*Education in Dubai is regulated with an intensity that surprises almost every first-time founder — plan for the process, not against it.*

---

Teaching and training others is one of the most natural businesses a skilled professional thinks of entering. The logic seems straightforward: find students, charge fees, deliver lessons. In Dubai, that logic runs directly into one of the most rigorous regulatory frameworks in the category — the Knowledge and Human Development Authority, known as KHDA. Understanding KHDA's role before you commit to a location, a lease, or a curriculum is not optional; it is the first task, and it shapes every decision that follows.

## 1 · What this business actually is in Dubai

Tutoring and training in Dubai divides into two clearly distinct operating models, and the distinction matters enormously from a regulatory standpoint.

The first model is the tutoring and training centre: a fixed premises, a named business, delivering structured educational or skills programmes to students or trainees. This might be a supplementary tutoring centre supporting school-age children in maths, English, or science; a language school; a professional skills training centre delivering certifications in business, technology, or vocational subjects; or an arts and enrichment programme. Any of these, operating from a physical premises and taking on enrolled students, requires KHDA approval. KHDA is the educational regulator for private education and training activities in Dubai, and its approval process is substantive — it involves an assessment of the curriculum, the qualifications of the instructors, the suitability of the premises, and the management structure of the institution. This is not a rubber stamp; applications are reviewed and can be rejected or require revision.

The second model is one-to-one private tutoring: an individual, without a commercial premises dedicated to the activity, providing tuition to individual students, typically in the student's home or online. This lighter-touch model sits outside the full KHDA institutional approval regime, though it is not entirely unregulated — a freelance permit or professional licence may be required depending on the individual's visa and activity status.

For most people contemplating this chapter, the question is which route they are actually building. A single tutor working with a handful of families is a freelancer. A centre with multiple instructors, a fixed premises, and enrolled students is an institution. The line matters, and being on the wrong side of it — operating a de facto centre without KHDA approval — is a compliance risk that regulators take seriously.

## 2 · Capital required

A small-to-mid-size tutoring or training centre — four to six teaching rooms, capacity for twenty to forty students — will cost between AED 280,000 and AED 650,000 to launch, depending on location, fit-out, and how smoothly the KHDA approval process proceeds. These are indicative figures at the time of writing.

**Licence, KHDA approval, and associated fees.** Budget AED 30,000–60,000 for the DET commercial licence, KHDA initial approval, application fees, and any inspection or assessment costs. KHDA approval may also require engagement of a curriculum specialist or submission of detailed programme documentation, which carries its own cost. Confirm current fee schedules with KHDA directly — they are subject to change.

**Premises deposit and rent.** KHDA has minimum space standards per student that the premises must meet. A suitable centre space of 250–600 square metres in a residential or commercial area: AED 90,000–180,000 per year. Confirm that the premises you are considering is approvable by KHDA before signing the lease — not after.

**Fit-out and equipment.** Classroom furniture, whiteboards or smart boards, student desks, a reception area, and appropriate signage: AED 50,000–120,000. The finish need not be luxurious, but it must meet the institutional standards KHDA expects to see on inspection.

**Staff and visas.** Two to four instructors and an administrator at launch: AED 8,000–14,000 per person for visa and setup costs in year one. Instructor qualifications — including degree credentials and teaching experience — will be verified by KHDA; budget time for credential attestation.

**Curriculum development and materials.** If you are building a proprietary curriculum rather than licensing an established one, budget AED 15,000–40,000 for development, translation (if required), and assessment design.

**Working capital.** A minimum six-month reserve: AED 60,000–100,000.

## 3 · Licence & jurisdiction

The DET commercial licence is the starting point and the foundation. But in this category more than almost any other in Part Two, the DET licence is not the operative approval — KHDA is.

KHDA holds regulatory authority over private educational institutions and training providers in Dubai. Any business entity that operates a premises-based programme of education or training for students must obtain KHDA's permit and comply with its standards on an ongoing basis. KHDA approval involves a multi-stage process: initial application and concept review, premises inspection, curriculum and instructor review, and final permit issuance. The timeline for this process can run from three to nine months depending on the completeness of the application and the category of training being offered. Rushing it does not accelerate it.

For training that leads to professional or vocational certifications, KHDA may also require that the qualifications being awarded are recognised by the relevant authority. If you are planning to offer, for example, IT certifications or professional qualifications accredited by an international body, the accreditation status of the programmes in the UAE context must be confirmed in advance.

The free zone route is not appropriate for a premises-based centre that serves the Dubai public. Mainland is the correct jurisdiction.

The lighter-touch alternative — one-to-one private tutoring — may be operable under a freelance licence or professional licence without full KHDA institutional approval. If this is your actual model, confirm the correct licence category with DET and KHDA before proceeding, as the boundary between freelance tutoring and an unlicensed training operation is one that regulators monitor.

## 4 · First three customers

Enrolments in a tutoring or training centre come from sources that are different in character from those available to a salon or a gym. The decision to enrol a child — or to invest in professional development — is a considered one, and the channels that drive it reflect that.

**School and community parent networks.** For child-focused tutoring, the most effective first channel is the parent community attached to the nearby schools. Dubai's expatriate families are intensely networked through school WhatsApp groups, community Facebook pages, and parent association meetings. A new tutoring centre that reaches these networks — through a clear, professional introduction, a genuine referral from one parent to another, or a partnership with a school that recommends private tutoring — finds its first students faster than any other method.

**LinkedIn and professional community for corporate training.** If your offer is professional skills, language, or vocational training, LinkedIn is the operating channel. A clear description of the programme, targeted to the professional community in the relevant sector, combined with direct outreach to HR managers and learning-and-development leads at local businesses, generates the corporate enrolments that anchor a training centre's early revenue.

**Trial sessions and open days.** Nothing converts a prospective student — or their parent — more reliably than the experience itself. An open day, a free trial lesson, or a taster workshop delivered at a high standard is marketing and sales in one. The conversion rate from a trial session at a good centre is significantly higher than from a digital ad. Use it early and often.

## 5 · Profit margins & cash cycle

A well-run tutoring or training centre achieves gross margins of 55–70% — the variable cost of a teaching session is primarily the instructor's time, and when sessions are grouped, the cost per student is low relative to the fee charged. Net margin, after rent, staff, visa overhead, and regulatory costs, typically runs at 16–26% in a stable operation with consistent enrolment.

The cash cycle varies by programme structure. Centres that charge term or semester fees in advance collect cash before the service is delivered, which is a structural advantage. Centres that invoice monthly or per-session have a shorter cash cycle but less revenue visibility. Where possible, structure fees to align with school terms — parents are accustomed to this rhythm and the advance payment matches the cost base.

The specific cash trap in this category is the pre-revenue period imposed by the KHDA approval timeline. You cannot enrol students before approval is granted, but the rent, the staff, and the fit-out costs are running from the day the lease commences and the team is hired. A centre that takes six months to receive KHDA approval — and this is not an unusual timeline — carries six months of fixed costs with no enrolment income. This is not a surprise to be managed in hindsight; it must be built into the capital plan from the outset.

Breakeven typically falls between twelve and twenty-four months for a properly funded centre, depending on how quickly KHDA approval is obtained and how efficiently enrolment builds. More than in any other category in this section, the timeline is set partly by the regulator, not just by the market.

## 6 · Top 3 mistakes

**Signing the lease before KHDA approval is confirmed.** This is the most expensive mistake in the category, and it is made with dismal regularity. A founder secures a premises, fits it out, hires instructors, and then begins the KHDA approval process — only to discover that the premises does not meet KHDA's space standards, or that the curriculum requires revision, or that the instructors' credentials need additional attestation. The cost of redesigning a fitted-out premises, or of sitting on a lease for months while the approval is renegotiated, is severe. Engage with KHDA at the concept stage, confirm the premises is approvable before signing, and complete the approval process before incurring heavy pre-opening costs.

**Underestimating the credential and documentation requirements.** KHDA applies serious scrutiny to the qualifications of instructors. Degrees, teaching certificates, and professional credentials must be attested — a process that involves official verification and can take weeks or months, particularly for qualifications issued outside the UAE. Founders who discover this after hiring instructors face a delay they cannot resolve quickly. Build credential verification and attestation into the hiring timeline, not as an afterthought.

**Building for too narrow an enrolment base without a fallback.** A centre that is designed for thirty students and needs twenty-five to break even has very little margin for an enrolment shortfall or a change in the catchment's demographics. Dubai's resident population is mobile; a community of families that exists when you open may be materially different eighteen months later. Diversify the student base by age group, subject, or programme type where possible, so that the centre does not depend on a single cohort or curriculum for its viability.

## 7 · Who's already winning

The successful operators in Dubai's private tutoring and training sector share a characteristic that is both obvious and under-appreciated: they take the regulatory process seriously rather than viewing it as an obstacle to be minimised.

The established tutoring centre that has been operating for five or more years in a fixed location typically carries a KHDA approval history that functions as a competitive advantage — parents and corporate clients treat it as a signal of credibility and longevity. These businesses invest in curriculum quality and instructor continuity, and they price to reflect both. They do not compete on the cheapest per-hour rate; they compete on outcomes and on the track record they can point to.

The professional training centre that holds international accreditations — whether a recognised language certification or a sector-specific professional qualification — wins by offering students credentials that travel. A KHDA-approved centre that also holds accreditation from an internationally recognised body has a client proposition that a non-accredited competitor cannot match.

The freelance tutor operating at the lighter end of the model wins through personalisation and availability — the ability to match precisely to a student's needs and schedule in a way an institution cannot. These operators rarely build large businesses, but many build very stable ones.

---

## 8 · Verdict

A tutoring or training centre suits the founder who has genuine teaching or curriculum expertise, who respects the KHDA approval process as a substantive part of the business setup rather than a bureaucratic formality, and who has the financial runway to sustain the business through a pre-opening period that may last six to nine months or more.

The KHDA framework is the defining feature of this industry in Dubai. It creates a genuine barrier to entry — which protects established operators and rewards those who do the process properly. It also punishes those who try to shortcut it.

If your model is one-to-one private tutoring at a small scale, the regulatory pathway is meaningfully lighter — but confirm the correct licence structure with DET and KHDA before operating, because the line between freelance tutoring and an unlicensed centre is one the regulator monitors.

**Bottom line:** Done correctly, a KHDA-approved training centre is a defensible, recurring-revenue business with real barriers to entry — but "done correctly" starts with the approval process, and that process demands time, documentation, and patience that the business plan must account for from the first day.
