PART ONE — FOUNDATIONS

# Chapter 4 · The money — capital, banking, and cash flow

*What you truly need, why the bank account is slower than the licence, and the cash cycle that decides whether a profitable business survives.*

---

## The licence is the smallest line

Chapter 1 made a claim that this chapter now has to honour with numbers: that the licence is the smallest line in your launch budget, and that a founder with a twelve-month business and three months of money already has a cash-flow problem on day one. Your company now legally exists — Chapter 3 saw to that. Whether it survives is a question of money, and money in a Dubai business has three parts that founders tend to underestimate in the same order every time: how much capital they truly need, how hard and slow the bank account is, and how the cash cycle can drain a business that is, on paper, profitable.

We will take them in that order, and we will keep the figures concrete. As always, treat every specific number as subject to change — verify the current thresholds and confirm the costs for your industry against the Part Two chapters and the ready-reckoner in Part Three.

## How much capital you truly need

Start by separating two numbers that founders routinely merge: the cost of *opening* and the cost of *operating until the business pays for itself*. The second is far larger, and it is the one that is left out.

The cost of opening is the visible list. A basic free zone licence advertised from around AED 12,500 is, as we have said, the smallest line. Around it sit the establishment card, residence visas for you and any staff, medical tests and Emirates ID, premises or a flexi-desk, a security deposit on those premises, insurance, fit-out or equipment if your business needs them, and the early spend on branding, a website, and signage. Even for a lean, service-based business with no physical footprint, these items together typically run into the tens of thousands of dirhams. For a business with a real premises — a restaurant, a clinic, a salon, a workshop — fit-out and equipment alone can dwarf every other line, and the Part Two chapters give realistic ranges industry by industry.

The cost of operating is the number that actually decides survival. It is your rent, salaries, your own living costs, marketing, utilities, and licence and visa renewals, multiplied by the months before the business reliably covers them itself. Chapter 1 set the planning figure: most businesses in this book take six to twelve months to reach a steady footing, and a runway covering both the business and your living costs across that period is the plan, not pessimism. So the honest capital question is not "what does the licence cost" but "what is my monthly burn, and do I hold enough to fund it — with margin — for the time the business will realistically take". Answer that, and you have a capital figure you can trust. Skip it, and you have a licence and a countdown.

## The corporate bank account — the slow, compliance-heavy reality

Here is the part of the setup that most surprises founders, and the reason this chapter sits where it does: opening a corporate bank account in the UAE is slower, more demanding, and less certain than obtaining the licence itself.

Be ready for the reality rather than annoyed by it. UAE banks operate under serious compliance and anti-money-laundering obligations, and they apply them. Opening an account is not a counter transaction; it is an underwriting decision. The bank will want your trade licence, your establishment card, your shareholders' and managers' passports and visas, and the company's constitutional documents — and beyond those, it will want to understand the business. Expect questions, and expect to evidence your answers: what exactly the company does, who its customers and suppliers are, where its money will come from and go to, what your background is, and — increasingly — proof of *substance*, meaning evidence that the company is a real operating business with a real presence rather than a name on a licence. A genuine office, local customers, and a coherent activity all help. A flexi-desk with no staff, no local footprint, and an activity the bank struggles to understand will draw more scrutiny.

Two expectations will save you stress. First, time: account opening commonly takes several weeks, sometimes longer, and it runs on its own clock, entirely separate from the licensing timeline in Chapter 3. Do not plan to invoice or receive funds the week you get your licence. Second, outcome: an application can be declined, and a decline is not necessarily a judgement on you — it may simply be that your profile does not fit that bank's current appetite. If it happens, you apply elsewhere with a cleaner, fuller pack.

Choosing a bank, then, is a practical exercise, not a branding one. Weigh the things that will matter every week: minimum balance requirements and the penalties for breaching them, account and transaction fees, the quality of online and mobile banking, how the bank handles international transfers if your business needs them, and — underrated — whether the bank has genuine experience with companies of your jurisdiction and size. A bank that routinely handles free zone companies like yours will move faster than one that does not. Prepare a clear, honest business profile, expect the questions, and treat the account as something to start early and pursue patiently.

## Tax registration — VAT and corporate tax

Chapter 1 dismantled the "tax-free" myth; this chapter turns the facts into obligations you must register for.

**Value-added tax** is charged at 5 per cent and has applied since 2018. Registration with the Federal Tax Authority becomes **mandatory** once your taxable supplies pass **AED 375,000** in a twelve-month period, and it is available **voluntarily** from **AED 187,500**. Once registered, you charge VAT on your taxable sales, you may reclaim VAT on qualifying business costs, and you file periodic returns on time — late filing and late payment carry penalties. Voluntary registration before you reach the threshold can be worth considering, particularly if your customers are themselves VAT-registered businesses and your input costs carry VAT you would rather reclaim; weigh it with an adviser.

**Corporate tax** is a federal tax on business profits, in force for financial years starting on or after 1 June 2023. The rate is **0 per cent on taxable profits up to AED 375,000** and **9 per cent on profits above that threshold**. A *qualifying free zone person* may, under specific conditions, retain a 0 per cent rate on *qualifying income* — but, exactly as Chapter 2 warned, that status is conditional, it is not automatic, and it is not a headline you can simply assume. Most businesses must register for corporate tax regardless of whether they expect to owe anything, and registration and filing obligations apply even at a 0 per cent outcome. Confirm your registration deadlines and your position with an adviser; the rules are still settling, and the specifics move.

The practical point under both taxes is the same: tax in Dubai is light by world standards, but it is now an administrative discipline you must keep, not a topic you may ignore.

## Bookkeeping discipline

That discipline rests on bookkeeping, and bookkeeping is no longer optional in the UAE. VAT returns, corporate tax filings, and most free zones' audit requirements all assume you keep proper, contemporaneous accounting records, and the law expects records to be retained for a defined number of years.

Treat clean books as infrastructure, not admin. From your first transaction, keep business money and personal money in separate accounts; record income and expenses as they happen rather than reconstructing them in a panic before a deadline; keep your invoices and receipts; and either use proper accounting software or engage a bookkeeper early. A small monthly cost here prevents three expensive outcomes: penalties for late or wrong filings, a painful audit, and — the quiet one — running the business blind, without knowing month to month whether you are actually making money. Which brings us to the distinction this whole chapter turns on.

## The cash cycle — profit is an opinion, cash is a fact

Chapter 1 stated the principle; here is the mechanism. **Profit is an opinion; cash is a fact.** Profit is what your accounts say you earned over a period. Cash is what is actually in the bank on a given morning. A business can show a profit and still fail, because it runs out of cash in the gap between the two — and Dubai has a particular, well-known version of that gap.

It is the trap Chapter 1 named, and it deserves to be spelt out. On one side, **rent is frequently paid annually in advance**, in one or two cheques — a large sum leaving your account at the start of the year, before the business has earned it back. On the other side, if you sell to other businesses, **your receivables can run 60 to 90 days** — you deliver the work, you invoice, and you wait two or three months to be paid. A profitable business can be crushed in the space between a year of rent paid up front and three months of revenue not yet collected. This has ended more Dubai businesses than bad ideas ever have.

You manage it deliberately. Hold a cash buffer sized to that gap, not to your average month. Negotiate rent terms — more cheques, even at a small premium, can be worth it for the cash relief. Invoice the day work is delivered, not the week after; agree payment terms in writing before you start; ask for deposits and staged payments; and chase overdue invoices as a routine, unembarrassed discipline rather than an awkward favour. Watch your cash position weekly. Every Part Two chapter analyses the specific cash cycle of its industry for exactly this reason — because the gap is not a rare misfortune, it is the normal shape of the work, and it must be planned for rather than survived.

## Funding the business — and paying yourself

Where does the capital come from? For most readers of this book, the honest and the healthiest answer is **bootstrapping** — funding the business from your own savings and then from its own revenue. It keeps you in full control, it forces the discipline that the rest of this chapter demands, and it does not put your launch at the mercy of a third party's timeline.

The alternatives each carry a cost. **Partners** can bring capital, skills, and shared risk — but a partner is a long-term relationship, and the ownership and decision-making terms must be written down clearly at the start, in the Memorandum of Association of Chapter 3, not assumed from goodwill. **Outside investors** are realistically relevant to a narrow band of high-growth businesses, chiefly in technology; for most industries in this book, investor funding is neither available nor appropriate, and pursuing it is a distraction. As for **bank lending to a new SME**, set expectations honestly: it is limited. Banks generally want a trading history, security, and a track record before they will lend to a small company, so a brand-new business should not build its plan on a loan it does not yet have. Plan to fund the launch yourself, and treat any external funding as a possibility for later, once there is a trading record to show.

Finally, the question founders are quietly anxious about: **when do you pay yourself?** The principled answer is that your own living costs should already be inside the runway you raised — Chapter 1 was deliberate about that — so that the business is never forced to pay you a wage before it can afford one. In the early months, keep your own draw modest and predictable, pay it as a defined, recorded amount rather than dipping irregularly into the company account, and let it rise only as the cash position genuinely supports it. Paying yourself too much, too soon, is simply another way of running out of cash. Paying yourself nothing and living on fumes is not heroic either — it is the burnout that Chapter 5 will warn against. The discipline is the same as everywhere else in this chapter: plan the number, record it, and respect it.

You now have the company, the bank account, the tax registrations, and a clear-eyed view of the cash cycle. What remains is the part no licence and no spreadsheet can supply — how you actually operate, day after day, year after year. That is the difference between founders who last and founders who fold, and it is Chapter 5.
