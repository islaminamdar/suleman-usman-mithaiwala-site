# Chapter 1 · Why Dubai

*The case for the city — made honestly, with the limits drawn in.*

---

## The question worth asking

Nearly everyone who sits across from me starts with the same question: *Can I make money in Dubai?* It is an understandable question, and it has a disappointing answer — yes. People make money here every day, in almost every industry in this book. But that answer is useless to you, because it is equally true that people lose money here every day, in those same industries. The question does not separate the two outcomes. It cannot tell you anything you can act on.

The question that can is narrower and less comfortable: *Does my business — with the capital I actually have, the time I can actually give it, and the temperament I actually possess — fit this city?* That is the question this book exists to answer, thirty-five times over, once for each industry in Part Two. But before we reach any industry, we have to be honest about the city itself. If Dubai is wrong for you, no chapter in Part Two will fix that. And if it is right for you, you should know precisely why — not because a video told you so.

## What Dubai actually is

Dubai is one of seven emirates that make up the United Arab Emirates. It is home to more than three and a half million residents, of whom roughly nine in ten were born somewhere else. Hold that figure in mind, because it shapes everything that follows: this is a city of arrivals. Almost everyone you will sell to, hire, partner with, or compete against came here on purpose, to work.

That is not an accident of history; it is the result of design. Dubai has comparatively little oil — far less than its neighbours — and it understood that early. So it built itself, deliberately, as a marketplace. First as a re-export hub, moving goods between continents. Then as a destination, drawing close to eighteen million international visitors a year. Then as a centre for finance, logistics, and professional services. The city sits at GMT+4, which means a single working day here overlaps the morning in East Asia and the afternoon in Europe. Jebel Ali is one of the largest ports on earth. Dubai International is among the busiest airports in the world for international passengers. None of this happened by luck.

It still doesn't. The government treats company formation, licensing, and visas the way other cities treat roads and water — as infrastructure. The current policy, the Dubai Economic Agenda known as D33, sets the explicit goal of doubling the size of the city's economy within a decade. You may find that ambition reassuring or excessive, but you should not ignore it, because it tells you what kind of environment you are entering: one that *wants* more businesses, and competes globally to attract them. That is the wind at your back. The rest of this chapter is about the parts of the journey the wind does not cover.

## The case, stated plainly

Here, then, is the genuine case for Dubai — six advantages that are real, each paired with the limit you should not forget.

**1. Location and logistics.** The three-continent working day is not a slogan; it is a practical asset if your business touches trade, distribution, or cross-border services. Goods clear quickly. Suppliers are close. Clients in three time zones are reachable before lunch. The limit: location helps a trading company or a consultancy enormously. It does very little for a neighbourhood salon, whose market is the four streets around it. Know which kind of business you are building.

**2. Real ownership.** For most of Dubai's history, a foreign founder on the mainland needed an Emirati partner holding a majority share. That requirement was removed for the large majority of commercial and industrial activities in 2021. In the free zones, full foreign ownership was always the rule. In plain terms: you can, today, own your company outright. That changes how you should think about control, profit, and succession — and we will return to it properly in Chapter 2.

**3. The tax position — stated accurately.** This is where honesty matters most, because the myth is loud. There is no personal income tax in the UAE; what you draw as salary or dividend is not taxed at the personal level. But "tax-free" as a description of *doing business* is no longer accurate. A value-added tax of five per cent has applied since 2018. A federal corporate tax of nine per cent has applied since June 2023 on business profits above AED 375,000 — profits below that threshold are still taxed at zero. Qualifying free zone companies can, under specific conditions, retain a zero rate on qualifying income. The honest summary is this: the tax burden in Dubai remains among the lowest of any serious commercial city in the world, but it is not zero, and a business plan that depends on it being zero is already wrong. Build your numbers on the real rates.

**4. The customer base.** Dubai's population is young, working, and — in meaningful pockets — willing to spend. Add the visitors, and you have a market far larger than three and a half million. The limit, and it is a real one: a city of arrivals is also a city of departures. Customers leave. Loyalty is shorter here than in a settled market. You will re-earn your customer base more often than you would elsewhere, and you must price and plan for that turnover rather than be surprised by it.

**5. Speed.** You can form a company in Dubai in days. Banking, premises, talent, payment systems, and suppliers are all available and competitive. For a founder, that speed is a gift — it shortens the distance between decision and trading. The limit is simply that the same speed is available to everyone, including the person who wants to copy you. Easy entry means crowded markets. Speed is an advantage only if you also have something the next entrant does not.

**6. Stability.** The UAE is politically stable and physically safe, and the dirham has been pegged to the US dollar for decades at a fixed rate. For a business owner, predictability is not a soft benefit. It means you can plan in a straight line — your costs, your pricing, and your currency exposure do not lurch from one quarter to the next. Many places in the world cannot offer that. Dubai can.

## The myths that cost people money

Against those six real advantages sit four beliefs that are not true, and that cost people money in a predictable order.

**The first myth is that Dubai is tax-free.** We have already dealt with the facts. The damage the myth does is subtler than a wrong number on a spreadsheet: it sets an expectation of effortlessness. Founders who arrive believing the city will cost them nothing are the same founders who are unprepared for the fees that appear at every step — licence renewals, visa costs, the establishment card, attestations, deposits. None of these is large on its own. Together, unbudgeted, they drain a launch.

**The second myth is that setup is the hard part.** It is the opposite. Setup is a transaction — you pay, you file, you receive a licence. It is the most certain part of the entire journey, which is exactly why so many founders over-invest in it. They spend the whole budget on a licence and a beautiful fit-out, open the doors, and discover they have no money left for the eighteen months in which the business is supposed to actually find its feet. The licence is the start of the work, not the proof of it.

**The third myth is that the city will bring you customers.** Dubai brings footfall to *Dubai*. It fills the airport and the malls and the calendar of events. It does not walk anyone through your door. That is your job, and it is hard enough that every single industry chapter in Part Two devotes a dedicated section to it, titled "First three customers." If a business cannot answer the question of where its first three customers come from — by name, by channel, by date — it does not yet have a business. It has a licence.

**The fourth myth is that everyone in Dubai is rich.** The Dubai of supercars and weekend brunches is real, but it is a thin and highly visible surface. The economy underneath it is built, like every economy, on small and medium businesses running on disciplined margins, and on a working population that budgets carefully and watches prices closely. If you set your prices for the Dubai of the postcard, you will price yourself out of the Dubai that actually shows up. Serve the city as it is.

## The real math

So let us put real numbers on the table, because the rest of this book runs on them.

A basic free zone licence can be advertised from around AED 12,500 a year. Take that figure and treat it as the smallest line in your launch budget, not the headline. The true cost of opening includes residence visas for you and any staff, an establishment card, medical tests and Emirates ID, premises or at least a flexi-desk, a security deposit, insurance, and — the line founders most often omit — working capital. By the time the doors open, the licence is frequently a fraction of what has been spent. A reliable instinct: whatever the licence quote, assume the genuine first-year cost is several times larger, and confirm the specifics for your industry using the figures in each Part Two chapter and the directory in Part Three.

Then there is the number nobody enjoys: runway. Almost no business in this book pays you back in its first month. Most take six to twelve months to reach a steady footing. A cash runway that covers both the business and your own living costs across that period is not pessimism — it is the plan. A founder with a twelve-month business and three months of money does not have a cash-flow problem somewhere in the future; they have one already, on day one.

Finally, the cash cycle — the rhythm of money in and money out — which matters so much that every Part Two chapter analyses it directly. Dubai has a particular trap here. You may be required to pay your rent annually, in advance, in one or two cheques. Meanwhile, if you sell to other businesses, you may wait sixty or ninety days to be paid. A profitable business can run out of cash in the gap between those two facts. It has happened to more Dubai businesses than bad ideas ever have. Profit is an opinion; cash is a fact. Manage the fact.

## Who Dubai is for — and who it isn't

This brings us to the only verdict that matters in this chapter.

Dubai is for the founder who arrives with a real runway, with a product or service the city genuinely wants, with the temperament to operate in a fast, crowded, and properly regulated market, and with the patience to do the unglamorous work — the licensing, the bookkeeping, the slow accumulation of customers — for longer than feels comfortable. Dubai rewards operators. If you want to build something and run it well, the city is, with few qualifications, on your side.

Dubai is not *yet* for the founder whose entire plan rests on the word "tax-free"; for the founder with three months of money and a twelve-month business; for the founder who expects income to be passive from the first week; or for the founder who has not tested whether anyone actually wants what they intend to sell. Note the word "yet." None of these is a permanent disqualification. Each is a sequencing problem. Extend the runway. Validate the demand. Correct the expectation. Then come. The city will still be here, and it will be a far better bet for the founder who arrives in that order.

## How to read the rest of this book

A short word on what you are holding, so you can use it efficiently.

**Part One** — these five chapters — covers the foundations every Dubai business shares, regardless of industry: the city itself, the jurisdiction decision, licensing, money, and the operating mindset. Read it in order. Each chapter assumes the one before it.

**Part Two** is the field guide and the heart of the book: thirty-five industries, six pages each, organised into eight categories — Food and Beverage, Personal and Lifestyle Services, Home and Property Services, Mobility and Transport, Trade and Commerce, Tourism and Events, Professional Services, and Tech and Specialised. Every one of the thirty-five chapters is built on the identical eight-section template, so you can compare any two industries directly: (1) what the business actually is in Dubai, (2) the capital required, (3) the licence and jurisdiction, (4) the first three customers, (5) the profit margins and cash cycle, (6) the top three mistakes, (7) who is already winning, and (8) the verdict. Read the categories that interest you; use the rest as a reference.

**Part Three** is the resource section — the directories, contacts, and checklists that turn a decision into an application.

One closing thought to carry into Chapter 2. Dubai is not a lottery ticket, and it was never built to be one. It is a well-engineered marketplace that pays out, reliably, to people who arrive prepared. Everything in this book is in the service of that one word — *prepared*. We begin with the decision that shapes every other one you will make, the choice that sets your costs, your ownership, your customers, and your licence in a single stroke: where, in legal terms, your business will live. That is Chapter 2.
