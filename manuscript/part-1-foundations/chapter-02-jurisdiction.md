PART ONE — FOUNDATIONS

# Chapter 2 · The jurisdiction decision

*Mainland, free zone, or offshore — the choice that sets your costs, your reach, and your customers.*

---

## The decision under the decision

Most founders believe their first real choice is *what* business to start. It is not. By the time you are reading this book you almost certainly have a business in mind, or a short list. The first choice that genuinely shapes your outcome is quieter and more technical, and it is the one this chapter exists to settle: *where, in legal terms, will your company live?*

In Dubai, "where" does not mean an address. It means a jurisdiction — a legal home that determines who may own your company, where you are permitted to trade, what your licence costs, how many visas you may sponsor, what premises you must hold, how a bank will read your file, and whether you must be audited. Three broad options exist: the **mainland**, the **free zones**, and **offshore**. They are not three prices for the same thing. They are three different legal environments, and choosing the wrong one is not a saving — it is a structural error you will spend money and months unwinding.

I have watched more launches damaged by this single decision than by any other. Almost always the damage traces to the same cause: the founder chose the cheapest package on a comparison website instead of choosing the jurisdiction that matched their customers. So before any number is discussed, hold on to one sentence. *The right jurisdiction is the one your customers can legally and comfortably buy from.* Everything below is the detail behind that sentence.

## The mainland

A mainland company is licensed by the **Dubai Department of Economy and Tourism** — the DET, formerly the DED. It is the "onshore" UAE company, the default for any business whose market is the people and companies of Dubai itself.

**Ownership.** This is the reform that changed the landscape. For most of Dubai's history a foreign founder on the mainland needed an Emirati partner holding 51 per cent of the shares. Since 2021, 100 per cent foreign ownership is permitted for the large majority of commercial and industrial activities. A limited set of strategic activities still carries local-ownership or local-agent requirements, so you must confirm the position for your specific activity — but for the typical restaurant, agency, trading company, or salon, you can now own the business outright.

**Where you may trade — the point that matters most.** A mainland licence lets you trade anywhere: with the public, with other businesses, across all seven emirates, and in government tenders. You can hold a shop on a main road, sell directly to walk-in customers, and invoice a government department. There is no internal wall between you and the Dubai market. For a business whose customers *are* the Dubai mainland, this freedom is not a luxury — it is the whole point.

**Cost shape, premises, and visas.** The mainland asks more of you. You will generally need a physical office or unit with a registered **Ejari** tenancy contract, and your visa quota is tied broadly to the size of that space. Expect first-year costs to run higher than a basic free zone package — licence, registered premises, and approvals together. In return you receive unrestricted market access and a visa allocation that scales with real premises.

**Banks and audit.** Banks tend to read a mainland company with a genuine office and a clear local market as straightforward to understand, which helps — though, as Chapter 4 will make plain, no account is ever simple to open. Audited financial statements are increasingly expected, and corporate-tax compliance makes proper books non-negotiable regardless.

## The free zones

Dubai has more than forty free zones, each a designated economic area run by its own authority with its own licence regime, its own activity list, and its own pricing. DMCC, IFZA, Meydan, Dubai South, DIFC, Dubai CommerCity, Dubai Internet City — these are not branches of one system. They are separate jurisdictions, and they differ from one another almost as much as they differ from the mainland.

**Ownership.** Simple and unchanged: 100 per cent foreign ownership has always been the rule in the free zones. That was their original promise, and it remains true.

**Where you may trade — read this twice.** A free zone company may trade freely *within its own free zone, with other free zones, and internationally*. What it generally may **not** do is sell directly into the Dubai mainland market as if it held a mainland licence. To reach mainland customers, a free zone company typically works through a mainland distributor, appoints a commercial agent, or opens a mainland branch — each an added cost and an added step. This is the single most expensive misunderstanding in Dubai company formation. A free zone licence does not entitle you to a shopfront on a mainland street, nor to sell directly to the mainland public. If your customers are the Dubai mainland, a free zone licence puts a wall between you and them, and you will pay — in margin, in agents, in branches — to get around the wall you chose to build.

**Cost shape, premises, and visas.** This is where free zones are genuinely attractive. A basic licence is advertised from roughly AED 12,500 a year, and many zones offer a **flexi-desk** — a shared or notional workspace that satisfies the premises requirement without a full office lease. Visa quotas are usually packaged with the licence, often a fixed small number of visas, and you can frequently buy more. For a consultancy, a trading company selling internationally, an e-commerce business, or a tech firm with no need for mainland walk-in trade, this lean cost shape is a real advantage. Treat the headline figure honestly, though — as Chapter 1 set out, the true first-year cost is a multiple of the licence once visas, the establishment card, Emirates ID, deposits, insurance, and working capital are counted.

**Banks and audit.** Banks have grown cautious about free zone companies that show a flexi-desk, no local market, and thin substance — they ask harder questions about what the business actually does and where its money comes from. A free zone company with real activity and real documentation is bankable; a shell-shaped one is not. Audit requirements vary by zone — some mandate audited accounts, some do not — and the corporate-tax regime, including the qualifying free zone person rules, makes proper accounts essential in any case.

## Offshore

The third option is the one most often misunderstood, because the word "offshore" carries baggage from elsewhere. In the UAE an **offshore company** — such as a JAFZA Offshore or RAK ICC company — is a specific, legitimate tool with a narrow purpose.

An offshore company is for **holding assets, owning intellectual property, and international structuring**. It can hold shares in other companies, own property where permitted, and hold a bank account. What it cannot do is operate inside the UAE: it has no licence to trade in the local market, and — this is decisive for most readers of this book — **it does not grant residence visas**. You cannot run a Dubai restaurant, agency, or shop through an offshore company, and you cannot get a residence visa through one.

So offshore is not an alternative to the mainland or a free zone for an operating business. It is a different instrument for a different job — a holding layer, a structuring vehicle. If your goal is to *operate* a business in Dubai and *live* here while you do, offshore is not your answer, and any adviser who offers it as the cheap route to a trading company is steering you wrong.

## How to decide

Now the method. The decision is not made by comparing prices. It is made by answering three questions honestly, in order.

**First: who are your customers, and where are they?** This question decides almost everything. If your customers are the Dubai mainland public or mainland businesses — a café, a salon, a clinic, a retail shop, a maintenance company, a firm chasing government contracts — you need a **mainland** licence. The free zone discount is no discount at all if it walls you off from the people you intend to sell to. If your customers are international, or other free zone companies, or you are running an online or consultancy business that never needs a mainland shopfront — a **free zone** licence will serve you well and cost you less. If you are not selling at all, but holding or structuring — that is the narrow case for **offshore**.

**Second: does the jurisdiction's activity list actually fit your business?** Every free zone licenses a defined list of activities, and the lists differ. A zone built for media will not cleanly license a food-trading company; a zone built for commodities may not fit a design studio. Before you commit, confirm that your exact activity — and every activity you expect to add later — is available in that jurisdiction. Founders who skip this step discover, a year in, that the natural next step for their business is not permitted on their licence.

**Third: what do your premises and visas genuinely require?** If you need to sponsor a team of fifteen, a flexi-desk will not carry that quota and a free zone may cap you below it; a mainland office sized to your headcount will. If you are a solo consultant who will never have walk-in customers, paying for a mainland retail unit is waste. Match the legal home to the real shape of the business — its people and its space — not to a brochure.

Answer those three, and the jurisdiction usually selects itself. Only then does price enter the conversation, and even then as a tie-breaker between suitable options, never as the thing that overrides suitability.

## The errors that cost the most

Three mistakes recur, and each is expensive enough to be worth naming plainly.

**Chasing the cheapest zone blindly.** The lowest licence price on a comparison site is a number with no context. It tells you nothing about whether that zone can license your activity, whether its visa quota fits your team, or whether its location walls you off from your market. A cheap licence that cannot reach your customers is the most expensive thing you can buy, because you pay for it and then pay again to work around it.

**Picking a zone whose activity list does not fit.** Founders fall in love with a zone's price or its address and force their business into an activity list that nearly fits. "Nearly" is the problem. The wrong activity classification limits what you can invoice for, complicates banking, and blocks the obvious expansion. Choose the jurisdiction whose list fits the business you are actually building, including its next two moves.

**Assuming a free zone licence allows a mainland shopfront.** This is the costliest error of all, and it is worth ending on. A free zone licence does not let you open a shop on a mainland street or sell directly to the mainland public. Founders sign free zone packages, then sign a mainland retail lease, and only at the licensing counter discover the two do not connect. Now they are paying for a free zone licence they cannot use as intended, a lease they cannot legally trade from, and a mainland branch or agent to bridge the gap. The wall was avoidable. It was chosen, unknowingly, on day one.

Get this decision right and the rest of setup is mechanical. Get it wrong and every later step inherits the error. With your jurisdiction settled, the next task is to turn that decision into a licence, a company, and a visa in your passport — the practical sequence, step by step. That is Chapter 3.
