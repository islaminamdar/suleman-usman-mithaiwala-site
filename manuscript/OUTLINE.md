# How to Start a Business in Dubai — Manuscript Bible

This file is the single source of truth for the manuscript: structure, voice, fact base, and the chapter template. Every chapter must conform to it.

## The book

- **Title:** How to Start a Business in Dubai: The Dubai Syndicate Way
- **Subtitle:** The Practical Field Guide to 35 Industries — From Restaurant to Real Estate, Salon to SaaS
- **Position:** Book two. Follows *The Dubai Syndicate Way*. Same voice, same brand, same 6×9 format, ~300 pages.
- **Structure:** Three parts.
  - **Part One — Foundations.** 5 chapters (Ch 1–5), ~40 pages. What every Dubai business shares.
  - **Part Two — The Field Guide.** 35 industry chapters (Ch 6–40), 6 pages each, in 8 categories.
  - **Part Three — Resources.** Directories, checklists, and tools.

## Voice & style

**Voice: the authoritative mentor.** Calm, structured, confident — a seasoned advisor walking the reader through carefully. Never breezy, never swaggering, never a hype-man. The reader should feel they are in steady, experienced hands.

- Address the reader directly as **"you."**
- Use first person ("I have watched…", "In my experience…") **sparingly** — for emphasis, not as a habit.
- **Honest and balanced.** Every advantage is paired with its limit; every opportunity with its cost. Never sell; inform. Credibility comes from telling the reader what does *not* work.
- **Concrete over abstract.** Real numbers, real steps, the real names of authorities. No motivational filler.
- **British spelling:** organise, centre, programme, colour, specialise. The noun is **"licence"**; the verb is **"license"**.
- Numbers: spell out one to ten in running prose; use figures for money, percentages, and data. Currency as **"AED 12,500"**.
- Headings in **sentence case**.
- Short to medium paragraphs. Bold lead-ins for list items. No bullet-point dumps in prose sections.
- Caveat all fees and figures — they change. Tell readers to verify current numbers and consult Part Three. Use phrasings like "at the time of writing", "expect this to move", "confirm the current figure".

## Fact base — keep every chapter consistent with this

- No personal income tax in the UAE.
- VAT: 5%, in force since 2018.
- Corporate tax: 9% federal corporate tax on business profits **above AED 375,000**, effective for financial years starting on or after 1 June 2023; **0%** on profits below that threshold. A **qualifying free zone person** may retain a 0% rate on **qualifying income** under specific conditions.
- Ownership: on the **mainland**, 100% foreign ownership is permitted for the large majority of commercial and industrial activities (reform effective 2021); a limited set of strategic activities still carries restrictions. In the **free zones**, 100% foreign ownership has always been the rule.
- Dubai has 40-plus free zones, each with its own authority and licence regime.
- **Offshore** companies (e.g. JAFZA Offshore, RAK ICC) are for holding, IP, and international structuring — no UAE operating presence, no residence visas.
- The dirham (AED) is pegged to the US dollar at roughly 3.6725.
- Dubai's population is more than 3.5 million, roughly nine in ten of them expatriates. The city draws close to 18 million international visitors a year.
- **D33** — the Dubai Economic Agenda — sets the goal of doubling the size of the city's economy within a decade.
- Key authorities (use the correct names):
  - **DET** — Dubai Department of Economy and Tourism (formerly the DED) — mainland licences.
  - Each **free zone authority** issues its own licences (DMCC, IFZA, Meydan, Dubai South, DIFC, Dubai CommerCity, Dubai Internet City, and others).
  - **GDRFA** — General Directorate of Residency and Foreigners Affairs — visas and residency.
  - **MOHRE** — Ministry of Human Resources and Emiratisation — labour and employment (mainland).
  - **FTA** — Federal Tax Authority — VAT and corporate tax registration.
  - **Dubai Municipality** — food, health, and building permits.
  - **Dubai Land Department / RERA** — real estate regulation and broker permits.
  - **RTA** — Roads and Transport Authority — vehicles, transport, and mobility permits.
  - **DTCM** (within DET) — tourism, tour operators, hotels, holiday homes.
  - **KHDA** — Knowledge and Human Development Authority — education and training institutes.
- A basic free zone licence is advertised from roughly AED 12,500 a year, but the true first-year cost is a multiple of that once visas, the establishment card, Emirates ID, premises or a flexi-desk, deposits, insurance, and working capital are included.
- Cash discipline themes carried from Chapter 1: rent is often paid annually in advance; B2B receivables can run 60–90 days; "profit is an opinion, cash is a fact"; a runway of six to twelve months is the plan, not pessimism.

## Part One — Foundations (Ch 1–5)

Flowing, essay-style chapters of roughly 2,400–3,000 words (~8 pages) each, with the chapter's own sentence-case thematic subheads (`## …`). Each builds on the one before and hands off to the next.

- **Chapter 1 · Why Dubai** — the honest case for the city. *(Written. Use as the voice reference.)*
- **Chapter 2 · The jurisdiction decision** — mainland vs free zone vs offshore: the trade-offs and how to choose.
- **Chapter 3 · Licensing, visas, and the setup sequence** — the practical mechanics from activity selection to Emirates ID.
- **Chapter 4 · The money — capital, banking, and cash flow** — what you truly need, the corporate bank account reality, tax registration, the cash cycle.
- **Chapter 5 · The operating mindset — the Dubai Syndicate Way** — how to operate, renew, network, hire, and how to read Part Two.

## Part Two — the 8-section chapter template

Every Part Two chapter is ~1,900 words (six 6×9 pages) and follows this exact structure. Open with a 1–2 sentence framing under the title, then eight numbered sections as `## 1 · …` headings:

1. **What this business actually is in Dubai** (~250 words). Define the business as it truly operates *in Dubai*, not generically — the local customer, the typical formats, what is different about running it here. Set honest expectations.
2. **Capital required** (~260 words). Realistic AED ranges for a lean start and a comfortable start. Break out the major line items (licence, premises and fit-out, equipment, stock, staff and visas, marketing, working capital). Give a defensible total range. Caveat that figures vary and move.
3. **Licence & jurisdiction** (~260 words). The activity and licence type; mainland vs free zone (and which) vs offshore; the relevant authority and any special approvals; whether physical premises are required; the practical recommendation.
4. **First three customers** (~250 words). Concretely — by channel, by method, by type — where the first three paying customers realistically come from. Tactical and specific. This section proves the business is real.
5. **Profit margins & cash cycle** (~250 words). Typical gross and net margin ranges; the cash cycle (money in vs money out); the specific cash trap of this business; realistic time to breakeven and payback.
6. **Top 3 mistakes** (~260 words). Three specific, common, costly mistakes founders make in *this* business in Dubai. Each a short bold-led paragraph.
7. **Who's already winning** (~200 words). A clear-eyed profile of the operators winning this niche today — positioning, model, what separates them from the strugglers. Describe strategies and archetypes; do **not** invent statistics about named companies.
8. **Verdict** (~170 words). A decisive close: who this genuinely suits, who should avoid it, the honest difficulty, and a final one-line **"Bottom line:"** sentence.

Each category opens with a short `category-intro.md` (~280 words): what binds these industries together, their shared dynamics, and how to read the category.
